# In-Person Visit Preparation
## For any institution — pathology, oncology, or research

---

## Who you are when you walk in

You are a mathematician.
You are not pretending to be a clinician.
You are not apologising for not being one.

A mathematician whose geometry predicted
something that 7,500 patients confirmed.
That is the introduction. Nothing more.

---

## The opening — say this or a version of it

*"I am a mathematician, not a clinician.
I applied a geometric framework to single
cancer cell data. The geometry told me which
two proteins define the identity axis of
breast cancer before I looked at any clinical
data. I wrote the prediction down. Then I
tested it in seven independent datasets with
seven thousand patients. It confirmed every
time. The test is two standard IHC stains
divided by each other. I need a pathologist
to run it on archived tissue to calibrate
the numbers. That is the only remaining
step."*

Fifteen seconds. Then stop talking.
Hand them the one-pager.
Let them read.

---

## The one sentence that anchors everything

> **"The geometry told me what to look for
> before I looked."**

If the conversation drifts, come back to this.
If they push back on credentials, come back
to this. If they ask why they should care,
come back to this.

Everything else is detail.

---

## What to bring — physically

- One-page summary: minimum 5 copies
- Partnership Proposal v3.0: minimum 3 copies
- Protocol Specification v3.0: minimum 2 copies
- A way to show the GitHub repository
  on a phone or laptop if asked

Hand them in that order.
One-pager first, always.
Do not hand them the protocol first.
A 20-page technical document handed to
someone who has not yet decided to care
is a conversation ender.

---

## How to read the room in the first 60 seconds

**They glance at it and set it down:**
They are not dismissing it.
They are deciding whether to engage.
Say nothing. Wait.

**They start reading:**
Stay quiet. Do not narrate what they
are reading. Let the document work.

**They look up and ask a question:**
This is the best possible sign.
Any question means they read enough
to want more.

**They look at your affiliation and pause:**
Do not fill the silence with an apology.
If they ask, say:
*"I am independent. Everything is in a
public repository with timestamps that
predate the confirmatory analysis.
The work can be checked by anyone
with a laptop in 20 minutes.
I am not asking you to trust me.
I am asking you to look."*

## Questions they will ask and what to say

**"Which antibodies?"**

*"FOXA1, clone D4E2, Cell Signaling
catalogue 53528. EZH2, clone D2H1,
Cell Signaling catalogue 5246.
Both rabbit monoclonal, both nuclear,
both already in routine clinical use.
The protocol covers Ventana, Dako,
and Leica platforms."*

---

**"How is it scored?"**

*"Standard H-score. Percentage of cancer
cell nuclei at weak, moderate, and strong
intensity, multiplied by 1, 2, 3,
summed to give 0 to 300 for each stain.
Divide FOXA1 by EZH2. Cancer cell nuclei
only — the protocol has a critical warning
about claudin-low stromal contamination
that would flip the classification if
stroma is included."*

---

**"What are the cut-points?"**

*"That is exactly what the calibration
study determines. The RNA-space values
are not transferable to IHC H-score units.
Cut-points are derived from scratch using
Youden J optimisation against PAM50
ground truth. That is what I need from
this collaboration."*

---

**"Why these two proteins?"**

*"FOXA1 opens luminal cell identity.
EZH2 silences it — it deposits repressive
marks directly on the FOXA1 promoter.
In Waddington landscape terms they are
the opposing forces on the primary
attractor axis. The ratio measures
which force is winning. That axis
maps directly onto both subtype and
treatment vulnerability. Two independent
groups published confirmation of the
same mechanism after I derived it —
Schade et al. in Nature 2024 and
Toska et al. in Nature Medicine 2017.
Neither knew about this framework."*

---

**"What is your institutional affiliation?"**

*"I am independent. The work is publicly
verifiable — timestamped before the
confirmatory analysis. I am not asking
you to trust me. I am asking you to
look at the work."*

Do not apologise. Do not elaborate.
Move on.

---

**"Why should we do this and not someone
else?"**

*"You do not have to. I am approaching
multiple institutions. The first one
to run the calibration study is the
first co-author on the publication.
The tissue is archived. The antibodies
are already in your lab. The protocol
is written. The only variable is
whether your institution wants to
be the one that validated this."*

---

**"This seems too simple."**

This is a good sign, not a bad one.
They are engaging with the biology.

*"It is simple because the geometry
found the right axis. The simplicity
is the result of deriving from first
principles, not a sign that something
is missing. ER IHC is also simple.
Two stains. One ratio. The mechanism
is what makes it meaningful."*

---

## What you are not doing

- You are not overselling
- You are not padding with credentials
  you do not have
- You are not speaking pathology jargon
  you do not own fluently
- You are not apologising for being
  independent
- You are not re-explaining after
  they have already understood

---

## If they say no or not now

Say this:

*"I understand completely.
Could you suggest a colleague who
might be better positioned to
evaluate this?"*

Then thank them and leave.

A referral from one pathologist
to another is worth more than
ten cold contacts.
The no is not the end of the
conversation. The referral is
the continuation of it.

---

## After the visit — same day

Write down:
- Who you spoke to, full name and role
- What they said verbatim if possible
- What they asked
- What their body language said
- Whether they kept the documents
- Whether they suggested anyone else

This record becomes your outreach map.
The patterns across multiple visits
will tell you which framing works,
which question comes up most, and
which type of institution is most
likely to engage.

---

## The disposition to carry in

You are not asking for charity.
You are not seeking validation
of yourself as a person.

You are offering the first institution
willing to run 300 slides the
co-authorship on a paper that
could change how breast cancer
is classified globally.

That is not a small offer.
Walk in like you know that.
