## Part 1: The Structural Problem
## You Are Navigating

Before the levers. The honest framing.

**The core constraint:**
You are an independent researcher with
no institutional affiliation. Every
funding mechanism, every conference
track, every tissue access pathway
was designed assuming an institution
exists behind the person applying.

**This is not fatal. It is a friction
problem, not a wall.**

Two things dissolve the friction:

1. A single institutional partner
   (which the in-person visit campaign
   is designed to produce)

2. A legal entity — OrganismCore
   as a registered 501(c)(3) nonprofit —
   that gives you institutional standing
   without requiring employment

The second is cheaper and faster than
most people realise.

SABCS is the single most important
breast cancer conference in the world.
40,000 attendees. Every major breast
cancer researcher attends or monitors
it. Abstract submissions open summer
2026 for the December 2026 meeting.

**The opportunity:**
You do not need IHC data to submit
a computational abstract to SABCS.

A poster presentation on the
computational validation — Waddington
attractor geometry, seven datasets,
~7,500 patients, zero contradictions,
timestamped prediction, protein-level
confirmation — is submittable now
as a computational/biomarker
methodology abstract.

What this produces:
- Citable, publicly recorded presentation
  at the world's premier breast cancer
  conference
- Physical presence at a venue where
  every breast cancer pathologist,
  oncologist, and researcher you want
  to meet will be in the same building
- The poster becomes a conversation
  tool that is infinitely more credible
  than a cold email
- Abstract published in Cancer Research
  supplement — a peer-reviewed journal,
  indexed, citable

**SABCS logistics for independent
researchers:**
- Abstract submission is open to all
- No institutional affiliation required
  on the abstract itself
- Registration is required to present
  (cost: approximately $800–$1,200
  for the full symposium)
- Poster printing: ~$150–$300 via
  their approved service

**Timeline:**
Abstract submissions typically open
June–July 2026 for the December
2026 meeting. Start preparing
the abstract now. It needs to be
written, not discovered.

as soon as OrganismCore is registered
as a 501(c)(3). Membership enables

## Part 4: Experiment.com —
## Scientific Crowdfunding

**What it is:**
The largest scientific crowdfunding
platform. ~$8.9 million raised across
~1,000 projects since 2012.
Cancer biomarker projects have
successfully funded on it.

**What the research showed:**
- Junior investigators and independent
  researchers have HIGHER success rates
  than established investigators
- The crowd applies different criteria
  than grant committees — story,
  clarity, and impact matter more
  than credentials
- Prior publication record does
  NOT strongly influence success

**What a successful campaign looks like
for your project:**

Target: $15,000 (consumables only)
Story: The geometry told me what
to look for before I looked.
Seven datasets. 7,500 patients.
One step remaining. 1.2 million
patients per year who could benefit.
$15,000 and 300 slides.

This is one of the strongest
crowdfunding narratives in cancer
research currently available to
anyone on the platform.
The equity argument — $50 test
vs $3,500 test, reaching patients
who currently receive nothing —
is emotionally and scientifically
compelling to a general audience.

**The constraint:**
Experiment.com campaigns work
best when the researcher has
existing audience or can drive
traffic to the campaign. A
scientific Twitter/X presence,
a LinkedIn profile, and a clear
public repository (which you have)
are the traffic drivers.

**The action:**
Build the Experiment.com campaign
page. Do not launch it yet.
Launch it as soon as you have
one institutional engagement —
even a letter of intent from a
pathology department that they are
interested in running the study.
That letter transforms the campaign
from "help me find a partner" to
"help fund a study already in motion."

## The Unexpected Local Lever

**What the research found:**
The Greater Chicago Komen chapter
raised $360,000 at the April 2025
Race for the Cure at Soldier Field.
Over $237,000 went to direct patient
assistance. The remainder funds
research and advocacy.

The Illinois chapter has explicit
research funding programmes and
accepts applications from
organisations focused on breast
cancer outcomes in Illinois.

**Why this matters:**
The Komen local chapter is not the
same as the national Komen grant
programme (which requires institutional
affiliation). The local Illinois
chapter has a community grants
programme that operates with
different criteria — local impact,
community benefit, patient reach.

A $15,000 community research grant
from Komen Illinois to fund the
FOXA1/EZH2 IHC calibration study
at a Chicagoland institution is
not an implausible ask. It is
exactly what their community
programme exists for.

**The framing for Komen:**
Not "fund my research."
"Help bring a $50 breast cancer
subtyping test to the 1.2 million
patients per year who currently
receive nothing, starting with
a calibration study right here
in Illinois."

**The action:**
Contact Komen Illinois Greater Chicago
chapter after OrganismCore is
registered as a 501(c)(3).
The contact is through:
`komen.org/community/illinois/greater-chicago/`

## Part 7: medRxiv Preprint —
## Visibility Before Publication

**What this is:**
medRxiv is the clinical and medical
preprint server. It is indexed,
citable, and read by clinicians
and researchers globally.

**What you can post today:**
The computational validation — all
seven datasets, the timestamped
prediction, the CPTAC protein
confirmation, the survival data —
is already complete and documented
in the Zenodo publications.
A medRxiv preprint presenting this
as a clinical methods paper would:

- Give you a peer-visible,
  indexed citation in the clinical
  literature before any journal
  peer review
- Create something a pathologist
  can look up independently before
  your in-person visit
- Establish timestamp priority
  in the medical literature
  (currently your priority is on
  Zenodo, which is not indexed
  in PubMed; medRxiv is)
- Make you findable to other
  researchers working in this space
  who will search for FOXA1/EZH2
  in breast cancer

**The framing of the preprint:**
Not "here is my computational work."
"FOXA1/EZH2 ratio as a universal
breast cancer subtype classifier:
computational validation in seven
independent datasets and IHC
calibration protocol for clinical
deployment."

That title is findable. It is
what researchers will search for.
And it positions the document as
the first step in a clinical
deployment pathway, not as a
standalone computational result.

**What medRxiv requires:**
No institutional affiliation required.
Free to post. DOI assigned immediately.
Not peer reviewed before posting
(that is the point — speed and
openness).

**The action:**
Draft the medRxiv preprint.
This is not a new writing project.
It is a synthesis of what already
exists across CS-LIT-1, CS-LIT-22,
and the master computational
artifact. The writing is mostly
done. It needs to be formatted
as a clinical methods manuscript.
