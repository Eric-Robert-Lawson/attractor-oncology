# In-Person Visit Preparation
## For any institution — pathology, oncology, or research

---

## Who you are when you walk in

You are a mathematician.
You are not pretending to be a clinician.
You are not apologising for not being one.

A mathematician whose geometry predicted
something that 7,500 patients confirmed.
That is the introduction. Nothing more.

---

## The one sentence

> **"The geometry told me what to look for
> before I looked."**

This is the only thing you need to hold
in memory before you walk in.

Everything else you already know.
You built it. It is not in your working
memory — it is in your understanding
of the work. The moment they respond,
the conversation generates structure
and you navigate from there.

---

## The opening

Say this. Then stop.

*"I am a mathematician, not a clinician.
I applied a geometric framework to single
cancer cell data. The geometry told me
which two proteins define the identity
axis of breast cancer before I looked
at any clinical data. I wrote the
prediction down. Then I tested it in
seven independent datasets with seven
thousand patients. It confirmed every
time. The test is two standard IHC
stains divided by each other. I need
a pathologist to run it on archived
tissue to calibrate the numbers.
That is the only remaining step."*

Hand them the one-pager.
Look at your own copy.
Wait for their eyes to come up
from the page.

That is the signal.
Not silence duration.
Not your internal sense of how long
it has been.
Their eyes coming up to meet yours.

Until that happens: wait.

---

## The silence rule

Silence after your opening is not
a gap. It is them walking toward
something.

You have already pointed.
Your job now is to let them arrive.

A person who arrives themselves
owns the understanding.
A person who receives a delivery
does not.

Every word added before their eyes
come up is you interrupting an arrival
that was already happening.

When silence becomes uncomfortable:
look at the document in your hand.
Not at them. At the page.
This is not avoidance.
It is patience made visible.

---

## The three anchors

If you lose the thread mid-conversation
— and it may happen — you have three
landmarks. When you find one you know
where you are.

**Anchor 1: The geometry told me first.**
Use when the conversation is about
how you found it.

**Anchor 2: The prediction was locked
before the data.**
Use when the conversation is about
whether it could be data dredging.

**Anchor 3: I am not asking you to trust
me. I am asking you to look.**
Use when the conversation is about
credentials or affiliation.

If you lose the thread, say:
*"Let me come back to what matters most:"*
Then use the relevant anchor.
This is not failure. It is precision.

---

## Answer what was asked

Only answer the question they asked.
Not the question behind it.
Not the question they might ask next.
Not the full context that would help
them understand the answer better.

The question they asked.
Then stop.

If they need more they will ask.
If they do not ask they did not need it.
Trust that.

---

## What to bring — physically

- One-page summary: minimum 5 copies
- Partnership Proposal v3.0: minimum 3 copies
- Protocol Specification v3.0: minimum 2 copies
- A way to show the GitHub repository
  on a phone or laptop if asked

Hand them in this order only.
One-pager first. Always.
A 20-page technical document handed
to someone who has not yet decided
to care is a conversation ender.

---

## How to read the room

**They glance at it and set it down:**
Not dismissal. Decision-making.
Say nothing. Wait for eyes.

**They start reading:**
Stay quiet. Do not narrate.
Let the document work.

**They look up and ask a question:**
Best possible sign.
Any question means they read enough
to want more.
Answer only what was asked.

**They look at your affiliation
and pause:**
Do not fill the silence.
If they ask, say:
*"I am independent. The work is in
a public repository with timestamps
that predate the confirmatory analysis.
Anyone can check it in 20 minutes.
I am not asking you to trust me.
I am asking you to look."*
Then stop.

## Questions they will ask

**"Which antibodies?"**

*"FOXA1, clone D4E2, Cell Signaling
catalogue 53528. EZH2, clone D2H1,
Cell Signaling catalogue 5246.
Both rabbit monoclonal, both nuclear,
both already in routine clinical use.
The protocol covers Ventana, Dako,
and Leica platforms."*

Stop there.

---

**"How is it scored?"**

*"Standard H-score. Percentage of
cancer cell nuclei at weak, moderate,
and strong intensity, multiplied by
1, 2, 3, summed to give 0 to 300
for each stain. Divide FOXA1 by EZH2.
Cancer cell nuclei only — the protocol
has a critical warning about stromal
contamination in claudin-low tumours
that would flip the classification
if stroma is included."*

Stop there.

---

**"What are the cut-points?"**

*"That is exactly what the calibration
study determines. RNA-space values are
not transferable to IHC H-score units.
Cut-points are derived from scratch
using Youden J optimisation against
PAM50 ground truth. That is what
I need from this collaboration."*

Stop there.

---

**"Why these two proteins?"**

*"FOXA1 opens luminal cell identity.
EZH2 silences it — it deposits
repressive marks directly on the
FOXA1 promoter. Their ratio measures
which force is winning. That axis
maps onto both subtype and treatment
vulnerability. Two independent groups
confirmed the same mechanism after
I derived it — Schade et al. in
Nature 2024 and Toska et al. in
Nature Medicine 2017. Neither knew
about this framework."*

Stop there.

---

**"What is your institutional
affiliation?"**

*"I am independent. The work is
publicly verifiable — timestamped
before the confirmatory analysis.
I am not asking you to trust me.
I am asking you to look at the work."*

Stop there. Do not elaborate.

---

**"Why should we do this?"**

*"You do not have to. I am approaching
multiple institutions. The first one
to run the calibration study is the
first co-author on the publication.
The tissue is archived. The antibodies
are in your lab. The protocol is
written. The only variable is whether
your institution wants to be the one
that validated this."*

Stop there.

---

**"This seems too simple."**

*"It is simple because the geometry
found the right axis. The simplicity
is the result of deriving from first
principles. ER IHC is also simple.
The mechanism is what makes it
meaningful."*

Stop there.

---

## If they say no or not now

*"I understand completely. Could you
suggest a colleague who might be
better positioned to evaluate this?"*

Thank them. Leave.

A referral from one pathologist
to another is worth more than
ten cold contacts.
The no is not the end.
The referral is the continuation.

---

## After the visit — same day

Write down immediately:
- Who you spoke to, full name and role
- What they asked — verbatim if possible
- What made them pause
- Whether they kept the documents
- Whether they suggested anyone else

The patterns across visits will tell
you which question comes up most,
which framing lands, which type of
institution is most likely to engage.

---

## The disposition to carry in

You are not asking for charity.
You are not seeking personal validation.

You are offering the first institution
willing to run 300 slides co-authorship
on a paper that could change how
breast cancer is classified globally.

That is not a small offer.

You pointed. The document is in
their hands. Their job now is to walk
toward what you pointed at.

Let them walk.
