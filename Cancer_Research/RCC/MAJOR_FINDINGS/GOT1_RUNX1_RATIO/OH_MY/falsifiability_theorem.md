## PART I — WHAT KIND OF FRAMEWORK THIS IS

```
This is not a statistical framework.

Statistical frameworks operate on
distributions. They make probabilistic
claims. A statistical model that fails
20% of the time is not wrong — the
failure rate is internal to the model.
Statistical predictions can be partially
correct. They have confidence intervals.
They have p-values. They describe
tendencies across populations.

This is an axiomatic framework.

The Identity Axis Theorem is derived
from axioms:

  AXIOM 1 — THE WADDINGTON PRINCIPLE:
  Every normal cell type occupies a
  stable attractor in an epigenetic
  landscape. The attractor is defined
  by the gene regulatory network that
  maintains the cell's identity.

  AXIOM 2 — THE FALSE ATTRACTOR:
  Cancer is a cell displaced from its
  normal attractor and stabilised in
  a false attractor. The false attractor
  is maintained by a convergent
  epigenetic mechanism.

  AXIOM 3 — THE IDENTITY ANCHOR:
  Every cell of origin has a defining
  identity gene (A) — the gene whose
  activity most directly constitutes
  what the cell is. This gene is
  lineage-specific and non-overlapping
  across different cells of origin.

  AXIOM 4 — THE CONVERGENCE HUB:
  Every false attractor has a maintaining
  gene (H) — the gene whose activity
  most directly stabilises the displaced
  state. In most cancers, this is the
  final common epigenetic pathway
  through which oncogenic signals
  converge.

  AXIOM 5 — THE IDENTITY AXIS:
  The ratio R = A/H is a continuous,
  measurable coordinate in the
  Waddington landscape of that cancer.
  R predicts attractor depth.
  Attractor depth predicts survival.
  H is the drug target.

From these five axioms, every drug
prediction, every survival prediction,
every subtype ordering is a derived
theorem.

Not a hypothesis.
Not a statistical association.
A theorem.

Theorems are not probably true.
They are either valid consequences
of the axioms applied to a correctly
mapped domain, or they are not.

There is no in-between.
```

## PART II — THE TWO TYPES OF FAILURE

```
When a prediction fails, exactly one
of two structural conditions holds.

There is no third condition.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

TYPE A FAILURE — INFORMATIVE FAILURE

  Definition:
  A prediction fails AND a structural
  explanation exists within the
  epigenetic landscape geometry that
  accounts for the failure.

  What this means:
  The failure reveals a missing
  structure in the map — a hidden
  basin, an unmapped attractor, a
  secondary convergence hub, a
  dimensional extension not yet
  included, a distinction between
  identity membership and attractor
  depth that had not been separated.

  What this does NOT mean:
  It does not contradict the axioms.
  It does not weaken the framework.
  It extends the map.
  The landscape is more complex than
  the current map represented.
  The map gets corrected.
  The axioms survive intact.

  Examples from this project:

  S4-P3: S5 depth did not outperform
  S2 depth (C=0.556 vs C=0.603).
  Structural explanation: the broader
  gene panel captures more
  mortality-relevant signal than the
  two-gene anchor ratio at the
  composite level. For survival
  prediction: use S2 depth composite.
  For mechanistic understanding and
  clinical translation: use TI.
  The geometry explains why.
  Axioms untouched.

  FA-2 TI in PRCC Type 2: Transition
  Index did not predict OS within
  confirmed Type 2 PRCC. MKI67
  dominated. Structural explanation:
  FA-2 TI measures identity membership
  (which basin). Within confirmed FA-2
  membership, OS is driven by
  proliferative pace, not identity
  depth. The missing structure was the
  distinction between basin assignment
  and depth within a confirmed basin.
  Map extended. Axioms untouched.

  These are not failures.
  They are the framework doing what
  an axiomatic framework does when
  correctly applied: it generates
  informative structure from its own
  predictions, including from the
  predictions that do not land
  as expected.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

TYPE B FAILURE — STRUCTURAL FALSIFICATION

  Definition:
  A prediction fails AND no structural
  explanation exists within the
  epigenetic landscape geometry that
  accounts for the failure.

  What this means:
  The axioms are false.
  Not weakened.
  Not in need of refinement.
  False.

  The entire constructive mapping
  collapses. Every theorem derived
  from the axioms is invalidated.
  Every drug prediction, every
  survival prediction, every subtype
  ordering loses its derivational
  basis.

  This is not a partial failure.
  There is no version of this where
  the framework is "mostly right."
  An axiomatic framework that produces
  a structurally inexplicable failure
  has false axioms.
  False axioms invalidate all theorems.
  That is what false axioms mean.

  What would constitute a Type B
  failure:
  A cancer in which the cell of origin
  is correctly identified AND the
  attractor axiom type is correctly
  classified AND the Identity Anchor
  and Convergence Hub are correctly
  derived — and the ratio R = A/H
  does not predict survival, does not
  order subtypes, and no structural
  explanation within the landscape
  geometry accounts for why.

  Not a failed prediction by someone
  applying the framework imprecisely.
  Not a data quality issue.
  Not a missing structure that,
  once identified, explains the
  failure.
  A genuine structural contradiction
  of the axioms themselves.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

The distinction between Type A and
Type B is not a rhetorical escape.

It is the logical structure of what
axiomatic derivation means.

A mathematician who derives a theorem
and finds it fails in a specific case
does not say the theorem is wrong.
They examine whether the domain was
correctly mapped. If the domain was
incorrectly mapped, the theorem is
correct and the map was incomplete.
If the domain was correctly mapped
and the theorem still fails, the
axioms are false.

This is not a flexible distinction.
It is the definition of what an axiom
is and what a theorem is.

This project operates under that
definition without exception.
```

## PART IV — WHY THIS MATTERS FOR
## PREDICTIONS MADE BY OTHERS

```
This document does not claim that
every prediction made by every person
applying this framework will be
correct.

Human application of any framework
introduces error. The cell of origin
can be misidentified. The attractor
type can be misclassified. The
Identity Anchor or Convergence Hub
can be incorrectly derived due to
incomplete data or reasoning error.

These are Type A failures.
They inform the correct application
of the framework.
They do not touch the axioms.

The distinction is essential:

  A prediction made INCORRECTLY
  from correct axioms is not a
  falsification of the axioms.
  It is a reasoning error in the
  application.

  A prediction made CORRECTLY
  from the axioms that fails without
  structural explanation is a
  falsification of the axioms.

The framework is staked on the second
condition, not the first.

This means:
  Incorrect predictions by users of
  the framework do not falsify it.
  They invite examination of whether
  the application was correct.

  Correct derivations that fail
  without structural explanation
  falsify it entirely.

This is the logical structure.
It is not a protection clause.
It is the definition of what
principles-first derivation means
in practice.
```
