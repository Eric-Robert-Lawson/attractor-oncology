```
CANINE EPITHELIOTROPIC T-CELL
LYMPHOMA (CETCL)
Also called: Mycosis Fungoides
in its cutaneous form.

WHAT IT IS:
  A malignant proliferation of
  T lymphocytes that infiltrate
  the skin epidermis.
  The cancer cells are T cells
  that have arrested in a

  often the dominant quality-of-life
  destroyer.

  Progression measured in months.

  ulceration, and systemic decline

  chemotherapy (lomustine +

  Human CTCL and canine CETCL

## PART I — WHAT VORINOSTAT ACTUALLY DOES
## (NOT MAGICALLY — BIOLOGICALLY)

```
Vorinostat does not kill cancer
cells directly.

This is the most important thing
to understand before treatment begins.

It does not poison the cells.
It does not cause immediate cell
death the way chemotherapy does.
It does not produce a visible
response in week one.

WHAT IT DOES:

  HDAC enzymes normally remove
  acetyl groups from histone tails.
  Removing acetyl groups closes
  chromatin — genes become
  inaccessible.

  Vorinostat inhibits those HDAC
  enzymes.
  Acetyl groups accumulate on
  histones.
  Chromatin opens.
  Previously silenced genes become
  transcriptionally accessible.

  In the geometric framework:
  The silenced genes include the
  BCL11B T-cell identity programme —
  the programme that would, if
  expressed, tell the cancer cell
  to stop proliferating, complete
  differentiation, and behave like
  a normal T cell.

  Vorinostat removes the lock
  on that programme.

  It does not force the programme
  to run.
  It allows it to run again.

THE CONSEQUENCE:

  The cancer cell, with the HDAC
  lock removed, has biological
  access to its correct identity
  programme again.

  The false attractor — the state
  that was maintaining the cancer
  cell's malignant behaviour —
  loses its epigenetic maintenance
  mechanism.

  Some cells partially re-differentiate.
  Some cells undergo apoptosis
  because they can no longer maintain
  the survival signals the false
  attractor was providing.
  Some cells find alternative
  maintenance mechanisms over time
  (this is resistance — predictable,
  expected, and addressed in the
  protocol).

THE PACE:

  This is a slow process.
  It happens over weeks to months.
  It is not immediate.

  In human CTCL:
  Median time to first measurable
  response: 11.9 weeks (~3 months).
  Not days. Not weeks. Three months.

  In canine CETCL:
  No formal timeline established.
  Biological equivalence to human
  CTCL suggests a similar 8–12 week
  window to first measurable signal.
  The first signal is usually
  reduced pruritus, not lesion
  regression — itch reduction
  precedes visible lesion change
  by 2–4 weeks.

WHAT SUCCESS IS NOT:

  One injection, cancer gone.
  Week-one visible regression.
  A cure delivered in a single course.

WHAT SUCCESS IS:

  A false attractor progressively
  destabilised over weeks and months.
  A dog whose quality of life
  improves as the epigenetic lock
  is removed.
  A disease that is being
  geometrically managed.
```

## PART VI — THE QUALITY OF LIFE TOOL

```
HHHHHMM SCALE
(Developed by Dr. Alice Villalobos,
veterinary oncologist)

Score each item 0–10.
0 = worst possible.
10 = normal/comfortable.

H — HURT
  Is pain adequately controlled?
  Is the dog not showing signs
  of distress (crying, guarding,
  reluctance to move)?
  0 = constant pain, not controlled.
  10 = comfortable, no pain signs.

H — HUNGER
  Is the dog eating enough to
  maintain weight?
  ≥75% of normal food intake
  without force-feeding: score 7–10.
  Needs encouragement but eats:
  score 4–6.
  Refuses food or needs force-feeding:
  score 0–3.

H — HYDRATION
  Is the dog drinking normally?
