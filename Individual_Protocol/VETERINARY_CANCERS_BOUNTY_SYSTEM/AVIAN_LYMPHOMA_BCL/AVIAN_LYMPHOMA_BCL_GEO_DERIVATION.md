FULL NAME:
  Avian B-cell lymphoma.
  A malignant neoplasm of the
  B-lymphocyte lineage in birds.
  The most common immunophenotype
  of avian lymphoma in budgerigars
  and psittacines generally.

SPECIES MOST AFFECTED:
  Melopsittacus undulatus
  (budgerigar):
    High incidence. B-cell
    immunophenotype most common.
    Body weight 35–45g.

  Nymphicus hollandicus (cockatiel):
    Regularly reported. Body
    weight 80–130g.

  Amazon parrots, African greys,
  and other large psittacines:
    Reported. Long-lived birds
    with extended tumour development
    windows. Body weight 300–600g.

  Raptors and passerines:
    Reported across a range of
    avian species. The germinal
    centre B-cell biology is
    conserved across birds.

ANATOMICAL DISTRIBUTION:
  Avian lymphoma presents as
  multicentric lymphoid infiltration.
  Birds lack conventional lymph
  nodes. The tumour presents as:

  Visceral infiltration:
    Spleen, liver, kidneys, and
    intestines are the most
    commonly infiltrated organs.
    Organomegaly — particularly
    splenomegaly and hepatomegaly
    — is the most common radiographic
    finding. Palpation of the
    enlarged organs may be possible
    in larger birds.

  Cutaneous lymphoma:
    A subset of avian lymphoma
    presents as skin masses —
    plaques, nodules, or diffuse
    thickening. This is more common
    in budgerigars. The cutaneous
    form has the most accessible
    response signal in the lymphoma
    subgroup — mass visible to
    the owner.

  Coelomic effusion:
    Advanced disease may produce
    ascites or air sac infiltration
    resulting in coelomic distension
    and respiratory compromise.

  Peripheral blood:
    A leukaemic picture with
    circulating lymphoblasts may
    be present. CBC with differential
    is an accessible monitoring tool.

CLINICAL SIGNS:
  Weight loss — progressive,
    often the first sign.
  Lethargy and reduced vocalisation.
  Abdominal distension — from
    organomegaly or effusion.
  Dyspnoea — from coelomic
    compression or air sac
    infiltration.
  Cutaneous masses or plaques —
    where the cutaneous form is
    present.
  Pale mucous membranes — from
    anaemia secondary to bone
    marrow infiltration or
    haemolysis.
  Polyuria — hepatic or renal
    infiltration disrupting organ
    function.

  Chlorambucil:
    An oral alkylating agent.

  The germinal centre B lymphocyte.

  The B cell enters the GC,
  diversifies its antibody, is
