## PART I — THE CANCER

```
FULL NAME:
  Avian nephroblastoma.
  A malignant neoplasm arising
  from undifferentiated metanephric
  blastemal cells of the avian
  kidney.
  The avian equivalent of human
  Wilms tumour.
  The most commonly reported
  neoplasm in budgerigars.

SPECIES MOST AFFECTED:
  Melopsittacus undulatus
  (budgerigar):
    Highest incidence. Body weight
    35–45g. The primary bounty
    target species for this entry.

  Other psittacines:
    Reported in cockatiels and
    other parrots, though at lower
    frequency than in budgerigars.
    Budgerigars account for the
    majority of reported avian
    nephroblastoma cases in
    pathology surveys.

ANATOMICAL DISTRIBUTION:
  Avian kidneys are situated in
  the synsacrum — the fused pelvic
  vertebrae — and are divided into
  three lobes on each side: cranial,
  middle, and caudal. They are
  retroperitoneal, deep within the
  coelomic cavity, fixed in position.

  The tumour arises within the
  renal parenchyma and expands
  within this fixed anatomical
  space. It cannot grow outward
  freely — it grows inward,
  compressing adjacent structures.

  THE SCIATIC NERVE:
    The sciatic nerve exits the
    lumbar plexus and passes
    immediately lateral to the
    avian kidney. This is the
    defining anatomical vulnerability
    of this cancer. As the renal
    mass enlarges, it compresses
    the ipsilateral sciatic nerve.
    The result is progressive
    unilateral leg paresis or
    paralysis. In most clinical
    presentations, the bird is
    brought to the vet because it
    cannot use one leg.

    CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE:
    The lameness is the entry
    point — the presenting sign —
    and the leg function recovery
    is the primary response signal.
    This is what the owner sees,
    what the vet treats, and what
    the framework targets.

CLINICAL SIGNS:
  Unilateral leg paresis or
    paralysis — sudden onset,
    progressive. The classic
    presentation. Most commonly
    the right leg, because the
    right kidney is slightly larger
    and more commonly affected in
    some series.
  Inability to perch — the bird
    sits on the cage floor.
  One foot hanging or dragged —
    owner notices immediately.
  Coelomic distension — as the
    tumour grows beyond the renal
    space, abdominal protrusion
    develops. May be visible from
    above as asymmetric fullness
    on one side.
  Weight loss — progressive.
    Often masked by the dramatic
    neurological sign.
  Dyspnoea — late stage, from
    coelomic compression.
  Polyuria — contralateral kidney
    compensation, or paraneoplastic.
  Hypercalcaemia — paraneoplastic,
    from tumour-derived PTHrP.
    Possible. May contribute to
    lethargy, polydipsia.

STANDARD OF CARE (EXISTING):
  Vinorelbine:
    Semi-synthetic vinca alkaloid.
    Mechanism: microtubule disruption,
    mitotic arrest. Administered IV
    at avian micro-dose. Variable
    and short-term responses
    reported. Does not address the
    differentiation block — the
    cells that survive remain
    blastemal and resume
    proliferation. Leg function
    improvement is transient if
    it occurs at all.

  Carboplatin:
    Platinum-based alkylating agent.
    DNA crosslinking, cell death.
    Limited use in avian oncology
    due to renal toxicity concern
    — particularly relevant when
    the tumour is within the kidney
    and contralateral renal function
    is the only remaining source
    of filtration. Used cautiously
    where no safer alternative
    exists.

  Supportive care:
    Meloxicam (NSAID) for pain and
    anti-inflammatory effect.
    Anti-nausea agents where
    indicated. Nutritional support.

  The gap:
    All existing treatments are
    cytoreductive — they kill cells
    that currently exist. None of
    them address why the metanephric
    blastemal cells cannot
    differentiate into committed
    nephron progenitors. The EZH2/
    H3K27me3 lock on the nephron
    differentiation programme
    remains intact in surviving
    cells. The cells resume
    blastemal proliferation when
    cytotoxic pressure is lifted.
    The tumour regrows. Leg function
    is lost again.

    The framework's claim is that
    adding tazemetostat — an EZH2
    inhibitor — will address this
    gap directly: it removes the
    epigenetic lock on the nephron
    differentiation programme,
    forcing surviving blastemal
    cells toward committed nephron
    progenitor fate, terminal
    differentiation, and cell cycle
    exit.

PROGNOSIS WITHOUT THE PROTOCOL:
  Guarded to poor. Median survival
  after presentation typically
  weeks to a few months with
  treatment. Untreated: weeks.
  Leg function may recover
  transiently with cytoreductive
  treatment if tumour shrinks
  sufficiently to decompress the
  sciatic nerve, but relapse and
  permanent nerve compression
  follows when the tumour regrows.
  There is no current treatment
  that addresses the identity axis
  failure.
```

```
  The metanephric blastemal cell.

  The metanephric blastema is the
  population of embryonic renal
  mesenchymal progenitor cells

  definitive kidney are formed.
  In normal kidney development,
  these cells exist transiently
  during embryogenesis and undergo
  a mesenchymal-to-epithelial
  transition (MET) to become
  committed nephron progenitors

  tubules, glomeruli, and capsule
  of each nephron.

  Once MET is complete and the
  blastemal cells have committed
  to nephron progenitor fate,
  they lose their mesenchymal
  identity and can no longer
  self-renew as blastema. The
  transition is irreversible under
  normal conditions. The blastemal
  progenitor pool is exhausted
  when nephrogenesis is complete.

  In avian nephroblastoma:
  A population of metanephric
  blastemal cells failed to
  undergo MET. They persist in
  the undifferentiated mesenchymal
  state. They continue to
  self-renew and proliferate as
```
