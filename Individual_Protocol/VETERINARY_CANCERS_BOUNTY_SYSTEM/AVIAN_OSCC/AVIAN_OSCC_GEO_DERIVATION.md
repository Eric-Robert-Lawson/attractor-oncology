## PART I — THE CANCER

```
FULL NAME:
  Avian Oral Squamous Cell
  Carcinoma. A malignant neoplasm
  of the squamous epithelium lining
  the oral cavity, beak (maxilla
  or mandible), choana, tongue,
  or pharynx of birds.

  The most common sites in
  psittacine birds:
  — Maxilla (upper beak): most
    frequent, particularly in
    budgerigars.
  — Mandible (lower beak): less
    common, similar biology.
  — Choana and oropharynx:
    less accessible, harder to
    detect early.
  — Tongue: rare but documented.

  All sites share the same cell of
  origin and the same geometric
  derivation. The document applies
  to all oral SCC in avian patients
  regardless of exact sub-site.

SPECIES MOST AFFECTED:
  Melopsittacus undulatus (budgerigar):
    The species with the highest
    documented incidence of oral
    and beak SCC in avian practice.
    Budgerigars are small (30–40g),
    meaning drug cost per treatment
    is minimal — this is the species
    with the best cost-to-impact
    ratio in the avian bounty system
    for this cancer type.

  Nymphicus hollandicus (cockatiel):
    Second most common. Larger body
    weight (80–130g) increases drug
    cost proportionally but remains
    within bounty-system budget.

  Amazon parrots (Amazona spp.):
    Reported, less frequent than
    budgerigars. Larger body weight
    (300–500g) increases cost.
    High owner investment and
    long-lived species makes this
    a high-value validation target.

  African grey (Psittacus erithacus):
    Reported, high owner attachment,
    exceptional intelligence creates
    strong owner motivation for
    treatment. Weight 400–600g.

  Other psittacines:
    The derivation applies to
    all avian species with oral SCC.
    The geometry is not species-
    specific. Body weight determines
    dose and cost.

WHAT IT IS:
  A malignant proliferation of
  squamous epithelial cells of the
  oral mucosa that have lost their
  TP63-governed squamous
  differentiation programme and
  are locked in a proliferative,
  undifferentiated false attractor.

  Normal oral squamous epithelium
  is a hierarchically organised
  tissue: stem-like basal cells
  divide and give rise to daughters
  that progressively differentiate,
  migrate toward the surface, and
  are shed. TP63 is the master TF
  governing the identity and
  maintenance capacity of the basal
  squamous layer. SOX2 collaborates
  with TP63 to maintain this
  identity.

  In OSCC, the normal hierarchy
  is lost. The basal-like programme
  (TP63, SOX2) persists but the
  differentiation drive is blocked.
  EZH2 accumulates aberrantly,
  H3K27me3 silences the
  differentiation programme, and
  the cells proliferate without
  progressing toward terminal
  squamous differentiation.
  The false attractor is a
  permanently basal-like, proliferative
  state that is self-maintaining
  via the EZH2 lock.

  COX-2 is upregulated in the
  tumor microenvironment, providing
  a pro-inflammatory signal that
  supports tumor growth and
  angiogenesis. This is the target
  of piroxicam — the existing
  clinical SOC for avian OSCC.
  Piroxicam suppresses this
  inflammatory maintenance signal.
  Tazemetostat suppresses the
  EZH2 epigenetic lock.
  Together, both layers of the
  false attractor are addressed
  simultaneously.

CLINICAL PRESENTATION:
  Visible beak mass or deformity:
    The most commonly noticed sign
    by owners. A proliferative,
    ulcerative, or eroding growth
    on the maxilla or mandible.
    May appear as a swelling,
    irregular surface change,
    discolouration, or frank mass
    on or within the beak.
    In budgerigars, any abnormal
    tissue growth on or around
    the cere or beak margins
    should be evaluated.

  Dysphagia (difficulty eating):
    The bird has difficulty grasping,
    cracking, or swallowing food.
    Seed husks may accumulate
    without being consumed.
    The bird may attempt to eat
    and drop food repeatedly.
    In budgerigars, weight loss
    may be rapid when dysphagia
    begins because of the high
    energy demand of their small
    body mass.

  Bleeding from the oral cavity:
    Friable, vascular tumor tissue
    bleeds on contact. Owners may
    notice blood on perches, cage
    bars, food dishes, or around
    the beak.

  Facial swelling:
    Bony invasion of the maxilla
    or mandible produces facial
    asymmetry and swelling visible
    from outside the cage.
    Bony lysis and remodelling
    secondary to infiltration.

  Foul odour:
    Secondary bacterial infection
    within the necrotic tumor mass
    produces a distinctive foul
    odour from the oral cavity.
    Owners frequently notice this
    before the mass itself.

  Nasal discharge:
    Maxillary SCC may invade the
    nasal passage or choana,
    producing discharge, sneezing,
    or nasal airflow obstruction.

  Voice changes:
    Changes in vocalisation quality,
    reduced or absent vocalisation.
    Budgerigars normally vocalise
    frequently — sudden reduction
    in vocalisation is a clinical
    signal.

  Weight loss:
    Measurable with a gram scale.
    Critical monitoring parameter.
    Any unexplained weight loss
    > 5% in a budgerigar is an
    emergency signal.

  Lethargy and reduced activity:
    Systemic compromise as the
    cancer progresses and nutrition
    deteriorates.

STANDARD OF CARE (EXISTING):
  Surgical debulking or excision:
    Where the mass is accessible
    and the bird is stable enough
    for anaesthesia. Rarely curative
    due to infiltrative invasion of
    beak bone. Recurrence is common.

  Piroxicam:
    NSAID with anti-tumour activity
    via COX-2 inhibition in SCC.
    Used empirically in avian
    OSCC based on case reports
    (Bennett et al., lovebird case;
    various avian oncology case series).
    Oral administration.
    Anti-inflammatory, palliative,
    and direct anti-tumour effect
    via suppression of COX-2-driven
    tumour microenvironment signalling.
    Addresses the inflammatory
    maintenance signal layer of
    the false attractor.

  Meloxicam:
    Alternative NSAID with similar
    COX-2 profile and better-established
    avian dosing. May be substituted
    for piroxicam where preferred
    by the attending vet.

  Carboplatin (rarely):
    Chemotherapy used in individual
    cases with systemic disease.
    High toxicity risk. Not standard
    first-line for small birds.

  Supportive care:
    Pain management, nutritional
    support (hand-feeding or soft
    foods if dysphagia is present),
    antibiotics for secondary
    infection, antifungals if
    secondary Candida present.

PROGNOSIS WITHOUT TREATMENT:
  Progressive local invasion of
  bone and soft tissue. Dysphagia
  progresses to inability to eat.
  Starvation, haemorrhage, or
  secondary infection. Death within
  weeks to a few months of onset
  of clinical signs.
  The alternative to treatment
  is death on a short timeline.
  Piroxicam gives partial
  palliative response and slows
  progression. The geometric
  addition addresses the EZH2
  epigenetic block that piroxicam
  cannot reach — exactly as
  deslorelin does not reach EZH2
  in the gonadal cases.

## PART III — THE GEOMETRIC DERIVATION

```
STEP 1 — CELL OF ORIGIN:
  The basal squamous epithelial
  cell of the avian oral mucosa.
  The stem-like progenitor cell
  of the stratified squamous
  epithelium lining the beak,
  choana, oral cavity, and tongue.
  It is TP63-positive, SOX2-positive,
  and normally gives rise to
  daughters that progressively
  differentiate and undergo
  terminal squamous keratinisation
  before being shed from the
  mucosal surface.
  In OSCC, this hierarchy is
  disrupted: the basal programme
  persists, the differentiation
  drive is blocked, and the cells
  proliferate without differentiating.

STEP 2 — AXIOM TYPE:
  Game-theoretical reasoning domain.
  The squamous basal cell's identity
  state is dynamically maintained
  between two competing programmes:
  (1) the proliferative basal identity
  programme (TP63, SOX2) and
  (2) the terminal squamous
  differentiation programme
  (involucrin, loricrin, filaggrin).
  In the normal tissue, a fraction
  of cells exit the basal programme
  and commit to differentiation at
  each cell cycle. This balance is
  what maintains epithelial
  homeostasis.
  In OSCC, the balance is broken:
  the basal programme is locked
  and the differentiation programme
  is silenced by EZH2. The cell
  cannot exit the proliferative
  attractor. This is the false
  attractor.

  Note: The avian oral mucosal
  environment is subject to
  mechanical trauma (beak use,
  hard seeds), UV exposure (in
  outdoor birds), and chronic
  inflammatory stimuli. These
  factors elevate the baseline
  cellular stress in oral mucosa
  basal cells, increasing the
  probability of EZH2
  dysregulation and TP63 programme
  instability.

STEP 3 — THE IDENTITY ANCHOR (A):
  A = TP63

  TP63 (Tumour Protein 63) is
  the master transcription factor
  of squamous epithelial basal
  cell identity across all
  vertebrates studied. It is
  what makes a squamous basal
  cell what it is. Its expression
  is what maintains the cells
  in the squamous epithelial
  programme.

  In normal squamous epithelium:
  TP63 is expressed in the
  basal layer. As cells differentiate
  and move toward the surface,
  TP63 is progressively downregulated.
  Its loss is necessary for
  terminal squamous differentiation.

  In OSCC: TP63 is maintained
  in a dysregulated state. The
  basal programme cannot be
  properly resolved. In the most
  aggressive or poorly differentiated
  SCCs, TP63 itself may be
  abnormally regulated, driving
  proliferation without the normal
  differentiation balance.

  SOX2 collaborates with TP63
  as a squamous lineage-survival
  co-factor. In the bounty system,
  SOX2 H-score in IHC functions
  as an additional confirmatory
  marker of the squamous basal
  false attractor, not as a
  separate drug target.

STEP 4 — THE CONVERGENCE HUB (H):
  H = EZH2

  EZH2 is the catalytic subunit
  of PRC2. In normal squamous
  differentiation, EZH2 activity
  is carefully balanced: it
  silences non-squamous gene
  programmes while allowing the
  progressive de-repression of
  differentiation genes as cells
  move from the basal layer toward
  the surface.

  In OSCC: EZH2 is overexpressed
  and hyperactive. H3K27me3
  accumulates on the promoters
  of terminal squamous
  differentiation genes (involucrin,
  loricrin, and related targets).
  The differentiation programme
  is silenced. The false attractor
  is self-maintaining.

  Published confirmation in HNSCC:
  EZH2 overexpression is documented
  in HNSCC as a driver of
  dedifferentiation and aggressive
  phenotype. Tazemetostat
  (EZH2 inhibitor) reduces H3K27me3
  on differentiation gene promoters,
  re-enables partial squamous
  differentiation, and produces
  anti-tumour effects in preclinical
  models.

  In the avian context: EZH2 is
  universally conserved. Its
  hyperactivity in avian OSCC
  is the predicted consequence of
  the same disrupted balance
  between proliferation and
  differentiation that is
  established in human HNSCC.

  The drug that addresses H:
  TAZEMETOSTAT (EZH2 inhibitor)
  FDA-approved for follicular
  lymphoma and epithelioid sarcoma.
  Being explored clinically in
  HNSCC. Inhibits EZH2 catalytic
  activity. Reduces H3K27me3
  on differentiation programme
  promoters. Re-enables squamous
  differentiation drive. Attacks
  the cell-intrinsic epigenetic
  lock that piroxicam cannot reach.

STEP 5 — THE RATIO:
  R = TP63 expression /
      EZH2 expression

  Measured by IHC as H-score
  of TP63 nuclear staining divided
  by H-score of EZH2 nuclear
  staining in tumour cells.

  High R (TP63 > EZH2):
    TP63 programme partially
    functional. Tumour is in
    a shallow false attractor —
    EZH2 has not yet fully
    overridden the TP63-mediated
    differentiation balance.
    Faster, stronger predicted
    response to tazemetostat.

  Low R (EZH2 >> TP63):
    Deep false attractor.
    TP63-driven differentiation
    balance heavily overridden
    by EZH2. Slower response.
    Longer treatment course needed.

  Note: In well-differentiated
  OSCC, TP63 expression tends to
  be higher and the tumour retains
  more squamous architecture.
  This likely corresponds to
  shallower R and faster response
  to tazemetostat.
  In poorly differentiated or
  spindle-cell OSCC, TP63 is
  often lower and EZH2 dominant —
  deeper R, slower response.
  This is a testable prediction
  from the framework.

STEP 6 — THE DRUG TARGET:
  Primary: PIROXICAM (existing)
    COX-2 inhibitor. Already used
    in avian OSCC as SOC.
    Addresses the COX-2-driven
    inflammatory tumour
    microenvironment maintenance
    signal — the extrinsic layer
    of the false attractor.
    Oral administration.
    (Meloxicam is an acceptable
    alternative where preferred.)

  Secondary (geometric addition):
    TAZEMETOSTAT
    Addresses the EZH2 epigenetic
    lock — the cell-intrinsic
    layer of the false attractor.
    Together: piroxicam suppresses
    the inflammatory support signal;
    tazemetostat removes the
    epigenetic maintenance mechanism.
    Both layers addressed.

STEP 7 — THE OBSERVABLE SIGNALS:
  Primary response readout:
    Reduction in visible oral/beak
    mass size.
    Observable by the owner and
    measurable by the vet with
    calipers at each visit.
    Baseline photo documentation
    at every visit creates a
    visible timeline.

  Secondary response readout:
    Reduction or cessation of oral
    bleeding. Owner-observable
    on perches and cage surfaces.
    Cessation of bleeding onto
    the cage surfaces is a strong
    positive signal that the owner
    will notice and report.

  Tertiary response readout:
    Radiographic reduction in
    bony lysis at week 8 (if bony
    invasion is present at baseline).

  Quaternary response readout:
    Improvement in eating behaviour.
    Owner-observable: the bird
    resumes normal seed-cracking,
    drops food less, gains or
    maintains weight.

  Functional response readout:
    Return of normal vocalisation
    activity. Budgerigars are
    among the most vocal companion
    birds. Return of chattering,
    mimicry, and contact calls
    is a functional QoL signal
    that the owner observes daily.

  Weight as objective readout:
    Gram-scale weighing at each
    vet visit. Weight gain or
    stabilisation after weeks
    of decline is an objective,
    equipment-independent response
    signal.

STEP 8 — THE DIMENSIONAL EXTENSION:
  If EZH2 inhibition alone is
  insufficient (deep attractor
  cases with very low R and
  no response at week 8–10),
  assess DNMT3A as a co-hub.
  A subset of oral SCCs may
  use DNMT3A-mediated CpG
  methylation to reinforce
  silencing of differentiation
  promoters in addition to the
  EZH2/H3K27me3 mechanism.
  If IHC shows high DNMT3A with
  poor tazemetostat response:
  consider azacitidine addition
  as second-line geometric
  extension.
  This is a second-line extension,
  not first-line. The primary
  protocol is piroxicam +
  tazemetostat.
