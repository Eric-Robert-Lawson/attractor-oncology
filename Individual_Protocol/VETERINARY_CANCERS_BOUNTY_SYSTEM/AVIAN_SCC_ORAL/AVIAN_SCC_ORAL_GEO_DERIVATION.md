## PART I — THE CANCER

```
FULL NAME:
  Avian oral and beak squamous
  cell carcinoma (SCC).
  A malignant neoplasm arising
  from the stratified squamous
  epithelial basal cells of the
  avian oropharyngeal mucosa,
  hard palate, and/or the
  rhamphotheca (beak) epithelium.
  The most common malignant tumour
  of the avian beak.

SPECIES MOST AFFECTED:
  African grey parrot
  (Psittacus erithacus):
    Highest reported prevalence
    among psittacines. Body weight
    400–500g. High owner investment.
    Long lifespan (40–60 years in
    captivity) — a diagnosis in a
    young or middle-aged bird is
    a decades-long companionship
    event for the owner.

  Amazon parrots (Amazona spp.):
    Yellow-naped, Blue-fronted,
    Orange-winged species all
    reported. Body weight 350–550g.

  Cockatoos (Cacatua spp.):
    Umbrella, sulphur-crested,
    Moluccan. Body weight 300–900g.

  Cockatiels (Nymphicus hollandicus):
    Lower frequency than the
    larger psittacines but relevant
    given high pet population.
    Body weight 80–130g.

  Other psittacines:
    Budgerigars occasionally
    affected but less commonly
    than the larger species.

ANATOMICAL DISTRIBUTION:
  The avian beak consists of:
    The rhinotheca (upper beak):
    dermal bone (premaxilla) covered
    by the rhamphotheca — a
    continuously growing, highly
    keratinised epidermal structure.
    The gnathotheca (lower beak):
    dermal bone covered by the
    rhamphotheca.
    The oral cavity: oropharyngeal
    mucosa, hard palate (choanal
    slit — the midline groove of
    the hard palate), tongue
    (lingual body), commissures.

  SCC arises in two main locations:
    1. BEAK SURFACE (RHAMPHOTHECA):
       The keratinising squamous
       epithelium of the beak
       surface. Mass appears as
       irregular proliferative
       keratinised tissue, erosion,
       or deformity of the beak
       contour. The beak grows
       crookedly, incompletely, or
       develops an abnormal hard
       irregular mass at the tip
       or along the lateral margin.
       Directly visible and
       photographable.

    2. ORAL CAVITY / PALATE:
       The non-keratinising
       stratified squamous mucosa
       of the oral cavity and hard
       palate. Mass appears as a
       sessile or pedunculated
       proliferative lesion or as
       an ulcerated erosive area.
       Visible on oral examination.
       Less immediately visible
       to the owner but detectable
       on examination.

  BONE INVASION:
    The beak is supported by bone
    (premaxilla, mandible). Advanced
    SCC invades the underlying bone,
    producing osteolysis. Bone
    invasion is a staging marker
    and a treatment-limiting factor
    for surgery.

CLINICAL SIGNS:
  Beak deformity — the most
    visible sign. Irregular growth,
    loss of normal beak contour,
    asymmetry, tip loss.
  Mass at beak — visible
    proliferative, keratinised, or
    ulcerated mass. Owner-visible
    at arm's length.
  Ulceration and bleeding —
    necrosis and haemorrhage from
    the tumour surface.
  Difficulty eating — pain
    interferes with prehension
    of food. Weight loss follows.
  Dropping food — the bird picks
    up food but cannot grip or
    process it due to beak geometry
    change or pain.
  Drooling or regurgitation —
    if the oral cavity is involved.
  Weight loss — secondary to
    pain and feeding difficulty.
  Reluctance to vocalise or
    decreased vocalisation — pain
    from oral involvement.

STANDARD OF CARE (EXISTING):
  Surgery (primary):
    Local excision where accessible.
    The fundamental limitation of
    avian beak and oral SCC surgery:
    complete margins are almost
    never achievable. The beak is
    a continuous keratinising
    structure over bone — a margin-
    free excision requires removing
    healthy beak tissue that the
    bird needs for feeding. In
    practice, partial debulking or
    the attempt at excision almost
    always leaves tumour at the
    surgical margin. Local recurrence
    rates approach 100% without
    adjuvant treatment. Surgery is
    palliative, not curative.

  Intralesional carboplatin:
    Carboplatin injected directly
    into the tumour mass at the
    beak. DNA crosslinking,
    cytotoxic to dividing cells.
    Reported case series show
    transient responses. Local
    recurrence is standard. Does
    not address the squamous
    differentiation block — the
    surviving cells are still
    ΔNp63α/SOX2-maintained basal
    cells in a false attractor.
    They resume proliferation.

  Meloxicam (NSAID/COX-2):
    Avian NSAID standard. Anti-
    inflammatory, analgesic. Some
    anti-tumour effect via COX-2
    inhibition of prostaglandin-
    driven tumour promotion. Not
    curative. Palliative. In
    mammalian SCC, piroxicam has
    demonstrated cleaner COX-2
    targeted anti-tumour activity —
    meloxicam is the avian-safe
    equivalent.

  Cryotherapy:
    Occasionally reported for
    small accessible lesions.
    Liquid nitrogen application.
    Tissue destruction without
    margin control — recurrence
    standard.

  The gap:
    All existing treatments are
    local and cytoreductive. None
    address why the squamous basal
    cells cannot undergo terminal
    differentiation and cornification.
    The ΔNp63α/SOX2 combination
    retains the basal cell identity
    programme in all layers. EZH2
    locks the cornification programme
    in the H3K27me3-silenced state.
    Surviving cells after any local
    treatment are still locked in
    the false attractor. They resume
    proliferation.

    Tazemetostat removes the
    H3K27me3 lock from the
    cornification programme
    promoters. The basal cells
    that survive local treatment
    are then able to undergo
    terminal differentiation —
    the programme that ΔNp63α/
    SOX2/EZH2 was preventing.
    Terminally differentiating
    cells cornify and die. They
    do not proliferate. The false
    attractor is dismantled.

PROGNOSIS WITHOUT THE PROTOCOL:
  Poor. Recurrence after any local
  treatment is the standard outcome.
  Survival times of months are
  typical. The beak continues to
  deform. Feeding becomes
  impossible. Euthanasia is the
  usual endpoint.
```
