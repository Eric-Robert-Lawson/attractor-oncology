## PART I — THE CANCER

```
FULL NAME:
  Canine Epitheliotropic T-Cell
  Lymphoma (CETCL).
  Also called: Mycosis Fungoides
  (cutaneous form).
  Also called: Canine Cutaneous
  T-Cell Lymphoma (CCTCL).

WHAT IT IS:
  A malignant proliferation of
  T lymphocytes that infiltrate
  the epidermis and dermis.
  The cancer cells are T cells
  that have arrested in a
  proliferative state and cannot
  complete their maturation into
  functional immune T cells.
  They accumulate in the skin,
  causing the characteristic
  plaques, depigmentation, and
  severe pruritus that define
  the clinical presentation.

BREEDS MOST AFFECTED:
  No strong breed predisposition
  documented for the cutaneous
  epitheliotropic form.
  Any breed can be affected.
  Middle-aged to older dogs
  are most commonly diagnosed.

CLINICAL PRESENTATION:
  Erythematous (red) plaques and
  patches on the skin.
  Depigmentation of skin,
  nasal planum, oral mucosa,
  and footpads.
  Severe pruritus — often the
  dominant quality-of-life
  destroyer and the owner's
  primary presenting complaint.
  Alopecia in affected areas.
  Thickened, scaly, hyperkeratotic
  skin as disease advances.
  Progressive ulceration with
  secondary bacterial/yeast
  infections.
  Eventually: lymph node involvement,
  systemic disease, transformation
  to large-cell lymphoma.

STANDARD OF CARE:
  Lomustine (CCNU): most commonly
  used. Median survival ~95 days.
  Prednisone: palliative symptom
  management.
  Retinoids: some palliative effect.
  Topical therapies: symptomatic.
  No currently approved curative
  treatment.
  No targeted molecular therapy.

PROGNOSIS WITHOUT EFFECTIVE TREATMENT:
  Median survival from diagnosis:
  approximately 6–9 months.
  Quality of life is severely
  compromised long before death
  due to intractable pruritus,
  pain from ulceration, and
  progressive disfigurement.
  Most dogs are euthanised for
  quality-of-life failure rather
  than acute systemic crisis.
```
