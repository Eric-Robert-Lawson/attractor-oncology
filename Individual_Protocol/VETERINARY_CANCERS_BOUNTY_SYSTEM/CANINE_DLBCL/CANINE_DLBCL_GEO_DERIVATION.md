## PART I — THE CANCER

```
FULL NAME:
  Canine Diffuse Large B-Cell Lymphoma.
  The most common subtype of canine
  lymphoma. Equivalent to human
  Diffuse Large B-Cell Lymphoma (DLBCL).
  Both are high-grade, aggressive,
  mature B-cell malignancies.

WHAT IT IS:
  A clonal expansion of large,
  transformed B lymphocytes that
  have escaped the normal germinal
  centre reaction and are locked in
  a proliferative, de-differentiated
  GC B-cell state. The cells have the
  identity of a GC B-cell in transit
  — a transient state that should
  last days, not decades — and they
  cannot exit it. The GC programme
  that was temporarily induced
  for affinity maturation has become
  permanent.

  In dogs, cDLBCL most commonly
  presents as multicentric lymphoma
  (peripheral lymph node enlargement,
  often all nodes simultaneously).
  It progresses rapidly without
  treatment: median survival untreated
  ~4–6 weeks.

CLINICAL PRESENTATION:
  Generalised peripheral
  lymphadenopathy (most common):
  submandibular, prescapular,
  axillary, inguinal, popliteal
  nodes all enlarged.
  The nodes are typically firm,
  non-painful, freely moveable.
  Splenomegaly and hepatomegaly
  in ~40% of cases at diagnosis.
  Constitutional signs: lethargy,
  reduced appetite, weight loss.
  Hypercalcaemia in a subset
  (~10–15%): polyuria/polydipsia.
  Occasional mediastinal involvement.

  Presentation tempo: days to
  weeks from first noticed
  lymph node enlargement to
  systemic signs.

BREEDS MOST AFFECTED:
  Boxer (particularly high risk —
  T-cell and B-cell lymphoma).
  Golden Retriever.
  Labrador Retriever.
  German Shepherd Dog.
  Standard Poodle.
  Rottweiler.
  Bassett Hound.
  Airedale Terrier.
  The disease is not rare in any
  large or medium breed.

INCIDENCE:
  Lymphoma = most common
  haematopoietic malignancy in dogs.
  ~1.3 per 1,000 dogs per year.
  Estimated 50,000–100,000 new
  canine lymphoma cases per year
  in the US.
  DLBCL = 30–50% of all canine
  lymphomas.
  Estimated 15,000–50,000 new
  cDLBCL cases per year in the US.
  This is the largest population
  among all veterinary cancer types
  examined in this framework.

STANDARD OF CARE:
  CHOP protocol:
    Cyclophosphamide (C)
    Doxorubicin / hydroxydaunorubicin (H)
    Vincristine / Oncovin (O)
    Prednisone (P)
    21-week protocol.
  Complete remission rate: ~85–90%.
  Median duration of first remission:
  ~10–12 months.
  Median overall survival: ~12–14 months.
  Most dogs relapse. Relapse disease
  is much harder to treat.
  Second remission rates with
  rescue protocols: ~40–50%.
  Median duration of second remission:
  ~3–4 months.

  The problem is not induction.
  The problem is the inevitability
  of relapse. The CHOP protocol
  achieves remission in 85–90% of
  dogs. Almost all of them relapse
  within 12–18 months.

  The geometry explains why.
  The CHOP drugs kill proliferating
  cells. They do not dissolve the
  EZH2 epigenetic lock. Residual
  cells that survive CHOP still
  have the PAX5 programme silenced
  by EZH2. They re-expand.

  Adding tazemetostat to CHOP
  or using it as maintenance therapy
  after CHOP-induced remission
  addresses the lock directly.
  This is the therapeutic logic.
  Not instead of CHOP. Alongside
  or after it.

AVAILABLE NON-CHOP OPTIONS:
  Rabacfosadine (Tanovea-CA1):
    FDA-approved for canine lymphoma.
    Alternative to CHOP for
    relapsed/refractory cases.
  Verdinexor (Laverdia-CA1):
    FDA-approved. Nuclear export
    inhibitor. Daily oral tablet.
    For relapsed/refractory cases.
  Lomustine (CCNU) + prednisone:
    Oral chemotherapy. Rescue protocol.
  Toceranib (Palladia):
    Some activity in relapsed cases.

  None of these address the EZH2
  epigenetic lock that is the
  geometric maintenance mechanism
  of the false attractor.
```

## PART II — THE CROSS-SPECIES LINK

```
HUMAN DLBCL:
  The most common adult non-Hodgkin
  lymphoma. ~25,000 new cases/year
  in the US.
  GCB subtype (germinal centre
  B-cell-like): ~45% of cases.
  ABC subtype (activated B-cell-like):
  ~45% of cases.
  Unclassified: ~10%.

  EZH2 Y646 gain-of-function mutation:
    Confirmed recurrent driver in
    human GCB-DLBCL (~20–25% of cases).
    Present in ~7–10% of all human
    DLBCL when both subtypes included.

  EZH2 overexpression without mutation:
    Much more common (~50–60% of all
    human DLBCL).
    High EZH2 protein / high H3K27me3
    correlates with aggressive disease
    and poor prognosis in both GCB
    and ABC subtypes.

  Tazemetostat in human DLBCL:
    FDA approved 2020 for EZH2-mutant
    relapsed/refractory DLBCL.
    Response rate in EZH2-mutant: ~69%
    (ORR in EZH2-mutant follicular
    lymphoma: higher).
    Response rate in EZH2 wild-type
    but EZH2-overexpressing DLBCL:
    lower (~20–35%) but real.

CANINE PARALLEL:
  EZH2 Y646 mutation: not confirmed
  as recurrent in canine DLBCL.
  EZH2 overexpression: confirmed.
  High H3K27me3: confirmed.

  The canine case is therefore
  geometrically parallel to the
  EZH2-overexpressing (non-mutant)
  human DLBCL population.
  The expected response rate with
  tazemetostat in cDLBCL is closer
  to the EZH2 wild-type human response
  range (~20–35%) than the mutant range.
  In the context of relapsed/refractory
  cDLBCL with median OS ~3–4 months
  on rescue protocols, a 20–35%
  objective response rate with
  tazemetostat is clinically significant.

  More importantly: the depth score
  (PAX5/EZH2 ratio) predicts which
  dogs are in the EZH2-active fraction
  — those with the highest EZH2
  H-score relative to PAX5 — and
  should therefore respond at rates
  closer to the mutation-positive
  human numbers.

  That is the predictive hypothesis
  being locked here.

## PART III — THE GEOMETRIC DERIVATION

```
DERIVATION PROTOCOL:
  Eight-step Identity Axis protocol
  as published in the Identity Axis
  Theorem (DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.18898788).

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

STEP 1 — CELL OF ORIGIN:
  Germinal centre B-cell (GCB).

  The GC B-cell is not a terminal
  cell type. It is a transiently
  de-differentiated state that
  normal B-cells pass through
  during the immune response.
  During the GC reaction:
    — BCL6 is expressed to suppress
      the normal B-cell identity
      programme (PAX5 targets) and
      activate the hypermutation
      and proliferation programme.
    — AID (AICDA) is expressed for
      somatic hypermutation.
    — The cell proliferates rapidly.
    — Selection occurs.
    — BCL6 is turned off.
    — The cell exits the GC as a
      plasma cell (antibody factory)
      or memory B-cell.
      PAX5 programme re-engages.

  In cDLBCL:
    The cell is stuck at the
    GC proliferative stage.
    BCL6 stays on.
    PAX5 targets stay suppressed.
    Somatic hypermutation continues
    without selection — acquiring
    further oncogenic mutations.
    The cell cannot exit.

STEP 2 — ATTRACTOR AXIOM TYPE:
  TYPE I — BLOCKED APPROACH.
  The cell is the B-cell lineage.
  It retains B-cell identity markers.
  It cannot complete the GC exit
  programme and differentiate into
  a plasma cell or memory B-cell.
  The B-cell identity programme
  (PAX5-governed) is suppressed
  by EZH2 acting downstream of
  constitutive BCL6 activity.
  The cell is stuck in a legitimate
  but transient state that has
  become pathologically permanent.

STEP 3 — IDENTITY ANCHOR (A): PAX5.
  (Paired Box Protein 5 /
  B-Cell–Specific Activator Protein,
  BSAP.)

  Derivation:
    PAX5 is the master B-cell
    identity transcription factor.
    PAX5 is expressed from the
    earliest B-cell committed
    progenitor through all mature
    B-cell stages.
    PAX5 knockout = no B-cells.
    PAX5 governs the transcriptional
    programme that makes a cell
    a B-cell: BCR signalling
    components, B-cell surface
    markers, B-cell survival genes.
    PAX5 is the thing a B-cell is.

    In the GC reaction:
    BCL6 partially suppresses PAX5
    activity to allow the transient
    GC de-differentiation. This is
    physiological — BCL6 directly
    represses PAX5 target genes
    during the GC reaction.
    When BCL6 is switched off at
    GC exit, PAX5 re-engages fully
    and drives plasma cell / memory
    cell differentiation.

    In cDLBCL:
    BCL6 remains on.
    EZH2 deposits H3K27me3 on
    PAX5 target gene promoters —
    locking the suppression in
    place at the chromatin level
    even in the absence of active
    BCL6 at those specific loci.
    The result: the GC
    de-differentiation that was
    BCL6-driven and reversible
    becomes EZH2-maintained and
    chromatin-permanent.

    PAX5 protein is present and
    expressed in cDLBCL cells —
    this is what makes it a B-cell
    and is used diagnostically.
    But PAX5's downstream targets
    (the re-differentiation programme)
    are EZH2-silenced.
    PAX5 is the Identity Anchor
    whose TARGET GENE PROGRAMME
    is blocked, not PAX5 itself.

    Confirmed in canine B-cell
    lymphoma: PAX5 IHC is the
    most reliable canine B-cell
    marker in use. Strong, diffuse
    nuclear positivity confirmed
    in FFPE canine lymphoma tissue.
    Human PAX5 antibodies cross-react
    with canine tissue (confirmed).

  A = PAX5.

STEP 4 — CONVERGENCE HUB (H): EZH2.

  Derivation:
    EZH2 is the hub that converts
    BCL6-initiated GC suppression
    of PAX5 targets into a permanent
    chromatin lock.

    Without EZH2:
    BCL6-mediated suppression of
    PAX5 targets is reversible —
    BCL6 dissociates and PAX5
    re-engages at GC exit.

    With EZH2 overexpression:
    H3K27me3 is deposited on
    PAX5 target promoters.
    BCL6 dissociation at GC exit
    is now insufficient to restore
    the programme — the methylation
    mark remains.
    The false attractor is chromatin-
    locked even without ongoing BCL6.
    This is why the cancer persists
    after BCL6 normalisation —
    EZH2 has taken over maintenance.

    EZH2 overexpression in canine
    DLBCL: confirmed in published
    veterinary literature.
    High H3K27me3 in canine DLBCL:
    confirmed in published literature.
    Both correlated with aggressive
    disease and shorter survival
    in dogs.

    EZH2 mutation (Y646):
    NOT confirmed as recurrent
    in canine DLBCL by current
    genomic studies. This is the
    critical distinction from human
    GCB-DLBCL where the mutation
    drives hyperactive EZH2.
    In canine DLBCL, EZH2 is
    overexpressed (more protein)
    rather than mutated (more
    active per molecule).
    The endpoint is the same:
    excess H3K27me3 on PAX5 targets.
    The mechanism of excess differs:
    overexpression vs hyperactivation.
    Tazemetostat inhibits EZH2
    activity regardless of the
    mechanism of excess.

  H = EZH2.

STEP 5 — IDENTITY AXIS:
  R = PAX5 H-score / EZH2 H-score.

  High R (PAX5 > EZH2):
    EZH2 activity is relatively
    modest. PAX5 programme is
    only partially suppressed.
    GC lock is shallow.
    CHOP may be sufficient.
    Tazemetostat as maintenance
    after CHOP remission —
    to prevent the residual
    EZH2 lock from driving relapse.
    Expected fastest and most
    complete tazemetostat response
    if escalation is needed.

  Low R (EZH2 >> PAX5):
    EZH2 overexpression is dominant.
    PAX5 programme is heavily
    H3K27me3-locked.
    GC lock is deep.
    CHOP will kill proliferating
    cells but the lock will persist
    in residual cells.
    High relapse risk.
    Tazemetostat combination with
    or after CHOP is the correct
    strategy. Not instead of CHOP.

STEP 6 — DRUG TARGET: EZH2.

STEP 7 — DRUG: Tazemetostat.
  Oral EZH2 inhibitor.
  FDA-approved for human EZH2-mutant
  DLBCL (2020) and EZH2-mutant
  follicular lymphoma (2020).
  Activity also demonstrated in
  EZH2 wild-type but overexpressing
  human DLBCL.

  Human dose: 800 mg BID oral.
  Canine dose: no established
  pharmacokinetic data published
  as of 2026-03-08.
  Starting dose for case report
  protocols: see IHC Depth Protocol
  document and veterinary oncologist
  consultation. Body surface area
  (BSA)-based extrapolation from
  human data is the recommended
  starting approach in the absence
  of canine PK data.

  Drug cost: ~$300–1,000/month
  depending on dog weight and
  compounding pharmacy.
  Compounding required for most
  canine weights — human 200mg
  tablets can be used directly
  for large breeds (~25–40kg)
  with appropriate half-tablet
  dosing. Compounded liquid or
  capsules for smaller dogs.

  Administration: with food or
  without food. BCS Class 2 drug —
  pH-dependent solubility. Administer
  with acidic food or immediately
  after eating to optimise absorption.

STEP 7 — PREDICTION (LOCKED
BEFORE LITERATURE CHECK OF DOSING):

  PRIMARY PREDICTION:
  "Tazemetostat added to CHOP (or as
  maintenance after CHOP-induced
  remission) will extend the duration
  of first remission beyond the
  standard 10–12 months in dogs
  with cDLBCL and confirmed high
  EZH2 H-score (EZH2 H-score > 150)
  on biopsy IHC.

  The mechanism: tazemetostat reduces
  H3K27me3 at PAX5 target gene
  promoters, partially restoring
  the B-cell re-differentiation
  programme, reducing the GC
  false attractor depth, and making
  residual tumour cells less capable
  of re-expanding into full relapse.

  DEPTH SCORE PREDICTION:
  Dogs with the lowest R (EZH2 >> PAX5)
  are at highest relapse risk after
  CHOP alone and are most likely to
  benefit from tazemetostat maintenance.
  Dogs with higher R (PAX5 ≥ EZH2)
  may achieve durable CHOP remission
  without tazemetostat. The depth
  score predicts who needs the second
  drug before they relapse.

  FIRST SIGNAL PREDICTION:
  The first detectable signal of
  tazemetostat activity in cDLBCL
  will be a reduction in EZH2
  H-score and H3K27me3 staining
  on repeat biopsy (if performed),
  before any clinical reduction
  in lymph node size, because the
  drug is dissolving the epigenetic
  lock rather than killing cells.

  RESISTANCE PREDICTION:
  Resistance to tazemetostat will
  manifest as relapse after initial
  depth score reduction, with EZH2
  replacement by alternative hub
  (KDM5C upregulation or BCL6
  re-amplification) rather than
  EZH2 re-mutation. Serial H-score
  monitoring should detect this
  as EZH2 H-score changes at
  relapse relative to response."

STEP 8 — LITERATURE CHECK:
