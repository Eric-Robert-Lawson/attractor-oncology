## PART I — THE CANCER

```
FULL NAME:
  Canine Oral Malignant Melanoma (OMM).
  Also: Canine Oral Melanoma (COM),
  Oral Malignant Melanoma (OMM).
  The most common malignant oral
  neoplasm in dogs. Not a rare cancer —
  this is the most frequently diagnosed
  oral tumour in the species.

WHAT IT IS:
  A malignant neoplasm of melanocytic
  origin arising within the oral
  cavity. Neural crest-derived
  melanocytes in the oral mucosa
  undergo oncogenic transformation
  and are locked in a proliferative,
  de-differentiated false attractor
  maintained by EZH2-mediated
  H3K27me3 deposition on MITF
  target gene promoters.

  The result:
  The melanocyte identity programme
  (MITF-governed: tyrosinase, Melan-A,
  DCT, MLANA, melanin synthesis) is
  progressively silenced. Cells lose
  pigment. The tumour becomes
  amelanotic. The loss of pigment
  is not an aesthetic finding —
  it is the visible sign of deepening
  attractor depth. More pigment lost =
  deeper EZH2 lock = worse prognosis.
  This relationship is confirmed in
  the published veterinary literature.

  In BRAF-mutant human cutaneous
  melanoma, the BRAF/MEK pathway
  drives initial proliferation and
  MITF suppression follows. In canine
  OMM, BRAF V600E mutations are rare
  — the primary driver is a direct
  epigenetic lock, not a kinase signal.
  EZH2 is the hub, not BRAF.
  This makes tazemetostat more likely
  to be the principal therapeutic
  lever in canine OMM than it is in
  BRAF-driven human melanoma.

CLINICAL PRESENTATION:
  Site: oral mucosa — most common:
    Gingiva (42–63% of cases)
    Lip / labial mucosa (15–33%)
    Palate
    Tongue (less common)
    Buccal mucosa
    Trigone / commissure

  Appearance:
    Pigmented form: dark brown to black
    mass, raised, often ulcerated,
    bleeding on contact.
    Amelanotic form: pale pink to
    flesh-coloured mass. Often
    misidentified as fibrosarcoma or
    squamous cell carcinoma on gross
    exam. Requires IHC to confirm
    melanocytic origin.
    Mixed pigmentation: partially
    pigmented, irregular colour.

  Growth pattern: rapid. Weeks to
  months from first visible lesion
  to significant local invasion.
  Bone invasion: common for gingival
  tumours. Lytic lesion on skull
  radiograph or CT.

  Behaviour: highly metastatic.
  Mandibular lymph node metastasis
  at diagnosis: 16–74% (range
  depends on detection method —
  histopathological detection
  identifies more than palpation).
  Distant metastasis (lung, liver,
  spleen, bone): 60–80% at necropsy.
  Most dogs die from metastatic
  disease even after local control
  is achieved.

BREEDS MOST AFFECTED:
  Dogs with heavily pigmented oral
  mucosa are overrepresented:
    Cocker Spaniel
    Chow Chow
    Scottish Terrier
    Standard Poodle
    Golden Retriever
    Dachshund
    Labrador Retriever
    Gordon Setter
  The pigmentation predisposition
  is consistent with higher melanocyte
  density in the oral mucosa of
  these breeds — more melanocytes =
  more cells at risk of transformation.
  No single breed has exclusive risk.
  Any dog with pigmented oral mucosa
  is at risk.

INCIDENCE:
  30–42% of all canine oral malignancies.
  Most common malignant oral tumour
  in dogs.
  Estimated 8,000–15,000 new cases
  per year in the US.
  Median age at diagnosis: ~11 years
  (range 5–17 years).

STANDARD OF CARE:
  Surgery:
    Wide surgical excision +/-
    partial mandibulectomy/maxillectomy
    for bone-invasive tumours.
    Achieves local control in early
    stages. Recurrence is common
    even with adequate margins.
    Median survival with surgery alone,
    stage-dependent:
      Stage I (< 2cm, no LN): 18–20 months
      Stage II (2–4cm, no LN): 12–15 months
      Stage III (≥ 4cm or LN+): 6–9 months
      Stage IV (distant mets): 2–4 months

  Radiation:
    Definitive or palliative protocols.
    Used for non-resectable tumours,
    incomplete margins, or as adjuvant.
    Response rate good for local control.
    Does not prevent distant metastasis.
    Median survival with radiation:
    ~7–12 months.

  ONCEPT DNA vaccine (xenogenic
  human tyrosinase DNA):
    USDA-conditionally-licensed for
    canine oral melanoma.
    Mechanism: human tyrosinase as
    xenoantigen breaks immune tolerance
    to self-tyrosinase.
    Survival benefit: modest but real
    in stage I/II with local control.
    Up to 18–20 months for responders.
    Not effective as monotherapy for
    gross disease.

  Immunotherapy (anti-PD-L1):
    Early studies (2024 consensus
    guidelines, Canine Chronicle 2024):
    radiotherapy + anti-PD-L1 antibody
    c4G12 showed improved OS vs
    radiation alone in advanced disease.
    Not yet widespread.

  Chemotherapy:
    Carboplatin, dacarbazine:
    Minimal benefit. Not standard.

  Toceranib (Palladia):
    Some anti-tumour activity in OMM.
    Not a standard recommendation.
    Evidence limited.

WHAT STANDARD OF CARE DOES NOT ADDRESS:
  The epigenetic lock.
  Surgery removes the primary tumour.
  Radiation kills cells in the field.
  The ONCEPT vaccine generates
  anti-tyrosinase immunity.
  Anti-PD-L1 blocks the immune
  checkpoint.
  None of these dissolve the EZH2
  H3K27me3 lock that:
    (a) drives the loss of immune
    visibility by silencing MITF
    targets including tyrosinase;
    (b) maintains the aggressive
    de-differentiated phenotype
    in residual cells after surgery;
    (c) enables metastatic outgrowth
    in the 60–80% of dogs who develop
    distant metastases despite local
    control.

  Tazemetostat addresses all three.
  That is the geometric therapeutic logic.
```
