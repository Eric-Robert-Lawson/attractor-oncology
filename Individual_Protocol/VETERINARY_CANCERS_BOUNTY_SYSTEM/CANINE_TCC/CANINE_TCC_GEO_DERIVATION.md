## PART I — THE CANCER

```
FULL NAME:
  Canine Transitional Cell Carcinoma
  (TCC). Also called: Urothelial
  Carcinoma (UC) of the bladder.
  The preferred current terminology
  in veterinary oncology is UC,
  mirroring the human field.
  Both terms are used in the
  veterinary literature. This document
  uses TCC as the common clinical
  shorthand.

WHAT IT IS:
  A malignant proliferation of
  urothelial luminal epithelial cells
  lining the bladder wall. The cancer
  cells are urothelial cells that
  have lost their luminal identity
  programme and are locked in a
  proliferative de-differentiated
  state. They accumulate in the
  bladder, obstructing urine flow
  and invading the bladder wall
  and adjacent structures.

  The trigone (where the ureters
  enter the bladder) is the most
  common location — in ~80% of
  cases. This location makes surgical
  resection difficult or impossible
  without compromising ureteral
  function. Most cases are therefore
  managed medically.

BREEDS MOST AFFECTED:
  Scottish Terrier: 18–20× normal risk.
  West Highland White Terrier: ~3×.
  Beagle: ~2×.
  Shetland Sheepdog: elevated risk.
  Wire Fox Terrier: elevated risk.
  Airedale Terrier: elevated risk.
  Border Collie: elevated risk.
  Female dogs: approximately 2× risk
  vs males across all breeds.

  If you work with Scottish Terriers
  regularly, TCC is not a rare event.
  It is an expected one.

CLINICAL PRESENTATION:
  Haematuria (blood in urine) —
  most common presenting sign.
  Pollakiuria (frequent urination).
  Stranguria (straining to urinate).
  Dysuria (painful urination).
  Recurrent urinary tract infections
  (UTI) that do not fully resolve
  with antibiotics — a key diagnostic
  clue.
  Incontinence in advanced disease.
  Reduced urine stream.
  In late disease: ureteral
  obstruction, hydronephrosis,
  renal failure.

  The haematuria and straining are
  often misdiagnosed as UTI for
  months before TCC is identified.
  Recurrent UTI in a middle-aged
  to older Scottish Terrier should
  prompt ultrasound.

STANDARD DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP:
  Urinalysis: haematuria, pyuria,
  may show neoplastic cells.
  Urine BRAF mutation test
  (CADET BRAF assay):
  ~85% sensitivity for TCC in dogs.
  Bladder ultrasound: trigone mass,
  irregular wall thickening.
  Confirmatory cytology or biopsy.
  Chest radiographs and abdominal
  ultrasound for staging.
  Regional lymph node evaluation.

STAGING:
  WHO clinical staging for
  canine bladder tumours:
  T1: superficial (mucosa/submucosa).
  T2: muscle invasion.
  T3: perivesical invasion.
  T4: adjacent organ invasion.
  N0–N2: lymph node status.
  M0–M1: metastasis status.

  Most dogs present at T2 or T3.
  Metastasis at diagnosis: ~20%.
  Regional lymph node involvement
  at diagnosis: ~30%.

STANDARD OF CARE:
  Piroxicam (NSAID, COX-2 inhibitor):
    First-line. 70% disease control
    rate. Median survival 6–7 months.
    Cost: $10–60/month.

  Piroxicam + mitoxantrone:
    ~35% objective response rate.
    Median survival 291 days.

  Piroxicam + vinblastine:
    ~25% objective response rate.
    Median survival ~290 days.

  Piroxicam + carboplatin:
    Comparable data.

  Toceranib (Palladia):
    ~25% response rate as single agent.
    Can combine with piroxicam.

  Surgery: partial cystectomy if
  tumour is non-trigonal. Rare.

  No currently approved targeted
  molecular therapy for the dominant
  DNMT3A-mediated epigenetic lock.
  Azacitidine (the geometry-derived
  targeted drug) has been tested
  in published veterinary studies
  with documented responses but
  is not standard of care.
```

## PART III — THE GEOMETRIC DERIVATION

```
DERIVATION PROTOCOL:
  Eight-step Identity Axis protocol
  as published in the Identity Axis
  Theorem (DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.18898788).
  Completed before any literature
  was consulted for drug target

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

STEP 1 — CELL OF ORIGIN:
  Urothelial luminal epithelial cell.
  The urothelium is a stratified
  epithelium lining the bladder,
  ureters, and urethra. It has
  a precise layered architecture:
  umbrella cells (surface),
  intermediate cells, and basal cells.
  TCC arises predominantly from
  the luminal urothelial cell
  population — the cells that
  directly contact urine and
  maintain barrier function.
  This cell type is conserved
  between dog and human.
  The urothelium has the same
  architecture, the same molecular
  identity programme, and the same
  cancer vulnerability in both species.

STEP 2 — ATTRACTOR AXIOM TYPE:
  TYPE I — BLOCKED APPROACH.
  The urothelial luminal cell has
  a strong defined identity:
  FOXA1-driven luminal differentiation,
  barrier function maintenance,
  uroplakin expression, limited
  proliferation.
  In TCC, the cancer cell retains
  urothelial lineage membership
  (it is still a urothelial cell)
  but cannot complete its luminal
  differentiation programme.
  The differentiation targets of
  FOXA1 are epigenetically silenced
  by DNMT3A-mediated CpG methylation.
  The cell is the right lineage
  in the wrong functional state.
  It is blocked from executing
  its identity, not displaced
  into a foreign one.

STEP 3 — IDENTITY ANCHOR (A): FOXA1
  (Forkhead Box A1 / Hepatocyte
  Nuclear Factor 3-alpha).

  Derivation:
    FOXA1 is the pioneer transcription
    factor of luminal epithelial
    identity across multiple epithelial
    lineages: breast, bladder, prostate,
    liver, lung.
    In the urothelium specifically:
    FOXA1 governs the luminal
    differentiation programme —
    uroplakin expression, barrier
    function genes, the entire
    cascade that makes a luminal
    urothelial cell what it is.
    FOXA1-knockout urothelium cannot
    maintain luminal differentiation.
    FOXA1 is the pioneer factor —
    it physically opens chromatin
    to allow the luminal programme
    genes to be transcribed.
    Without FOXA1 activity, luminal
    urothelial identity collapses.

    In TCC, FOXA1 expression is
    reduced or its targets are
    methylated, preventing it from
    executing the luminal programme
    even when the protein is present.
    This is the blocked approach:
    FOXA1 exists but cannot reach
    its targets through the
    DNMT3A methylation barrier.

    FOXA1 is confirmed expressed
    in canine urothelial tissue
    (published veterinary pathology
    literature).
    Cross-reactivity of human FOXA1
    antibodies with canine tissue
    is confirmed.

STEP 4 — CONVERGENCE HUB (H):
  PRIMARY HUB: DNMT3A
  (DNA methyltransferase 3A).

  Derivation:
    DNMT3A is the de novo DNA
    methyltransferase responsible
    for establishing new CpG
    methylation patterns.
    In TCC, DNMT3A deposits
    methylation on the CpG islands
    of FOXA1 target gene promoters —
    silencing the luminal
    differentiation programme at the
    DNA level.
    This is a deeper lock than
    histone modification alone:
    DNA methylation is heritable
    through cell division and
    requires active demethylation
    to reverse.
    The cancer uses DNMT3A to
    permanently silence the FOXA1
    luminal programme and lock
    the cells in the proliferative
    de-differentiated state.

    Azacitidine (5-azacytidine)
    is a nucleoside analogue that
    is incorporated into DNA during
    replication and irreversibly
    inhibits DNMT3A (and DNMT1)
    by forming a covalent trap.
    The result: progressive
    loss of CpG methylation with
    each cell division.
    The FOXA1 luminal programme
    promoters are demethylated.
    FOXA1 can now access its targets.
    The luminal differentiation
    programme re-engages.
    The false attractor loses its
    DNA methylation maintenance.

  SECONDARY HUB: EZH2
    In a subset of TCC cases,
    particularly in more advanced
    or deeply de-differentiated
    disease, EZH2-mediated H3K27me3
    silencing operates alongside
    or instead of DNMT3A methylation
    as the dominant Convergence Hub.
    This is detected by the
    IHC depth test:
    if EZH2 H-score is high
    relative to FOXA1 and DNMT3A
    is only moderately elevated,
    the primary hub is EZH2.
    Drug: tazemetostat in this case,
    not azacitidine.
    The IHC protocol determines
    which hub is dominant before
    drug selection.

STEP 5 — IDENTITY AXIS:
  R = FOXA1 expression /
      DNMT3A expression

  (Where DNMT3A is confirmed as
  primary hub by IHC.)

  Interpretation:
    High R (FOXA1 > DNMT3A):
    Shallow DNMT3A lock.
    FOXA1 still relatively active.
    Luminal programme partially
    accessible.
    Piroxicam alone may maintain
    disease control.
    Azacitidine escalation will
    be most effective here.

    Low R (DNMT3A >> FOXA1):
    Deep DNMT3A lock.
    FOXA1 programme heavily
    methylated.
    Piroxicam insufficient alone.
    Azacitidine escalation is the
    correct next step.
    Response may be slower as
    demethylation is progressive
    (requires cell divisions to
    complete).

    DNMT3A low / EZH2 high:
    Primary hub is EZH2, not DNMT3A.
    Azacitidine is incorrect drug
    for this patient.
    Tazemetostat is the correct drug.
    This distinction is why the IHC
    depth test is mandatory.

STEP 6 — DRUG TARGET:
  Primary: DNMT3A
  (in DNMT3A-dominant cases).
  Secondary: EZH2
  (in EZH2-dominant cases).
  Anti-proliferative co-drug: COX-2
  (via piroxicam, already in use).

STEP 7 — DRUGS:
  TIER 1 — IMMEDIATE START:
    Piroxicam.
    COX-2 inhibitor, NSAID.
    $10–60/month.
    Standard first-line drug.
    Already used. Already documented.
    Not the geometric drug.
    The inflammatory scaffold
    removal while the epigenetic
    drug is evaluated.

  TIER 2 — AFTER IHC DEPTH TEST:
    DNMT3A-dominant:
      Azacitidine, compounded.
      Hypomethylating agent.
      ~$300–900/cycle depending
      on weight and pharmacy.
      Targets the DNMT3A lock directly.

    EZH2-dominant:
      Tazemetostat, compounded.
      EZH2 inhibitor.
      ~$300–1,000/month.
      Targets the PRC2 arm directly.

STEP 7 — PREDICTION (LOCKED BEFORE
LITERATURE CHECK):

  PRIMARY PREDICTION:
  "Azacitidine added to piroxicam
  will produce objective response
  (partial remission or disease
  control beyond piroxicam baseline)
  in canine TCC patients with
  confirmed DNMT3A-dominant depth

  The response mechanism is
```
