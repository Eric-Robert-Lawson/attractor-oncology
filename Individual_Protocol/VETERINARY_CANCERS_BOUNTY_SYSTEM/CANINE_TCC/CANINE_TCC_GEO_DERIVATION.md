## PART I — THE CANCER

```
FULL NAME:
  Canine Transitional Cell Carcinoma
  (TCC) / Urothelial Carcinoma (UC).
  These two names refer to the same
  disease. TCC is the older term,
  still widely used in veterinary
  oncology. UC is the WHO-preferred
  current human oncology term.
  Both are used in the veterinary
  literature. This document uses TCC.

WHAT IT IS:
  A malignant proliferation of
  urothelial (transitional) epithelial
  cells lining the bladder, and
  less commonly the urethra, ureters,
  prostate, or vagina.
  The cancer cells are urothelial
  cells that have lost their
  differentiation programme and
  become locked in a proliferative
  false attractor.
  Most commonly affects the trigone
  region of the bladder — the
  anatomical area where the two
  ureters enter and the urethra exits.
  This location makes complete surgical
  resection nearly impossible.

BREEDS MOST AFFECTED:
  Scottish Terrier: 18–20× elevated risk.
    This is the highest breed-specific
    cancer risk documented for any
    cancer in any breed.
  West Highland White Terrier: elevated.
  Beagle: elevated.
  Shetland Sheepdog: elevated.
  Wire Fox Terrier: elevated.
  Any breed can be affected, but
  terrier breeds and females are
  disproportionately represented.

SEX:
  Intact and spayed females: 2× risk
  compared to intact males.
  Neutered males: intermediate risk.

CLINICAL PRESENTATION:
  Haematuria — blood in urine.
  Stranguria — straining to urinate,
  frequent attempts with little output.
  Dysuria — painful urination.
  Pollakiuria — abnormally frequent
  urination in small amounts.
  Urinary tract infection signs —
  secondary bacterial infection is
  common and can mask the primary
  cancer.
  In advanced disease: hindlimb
  weakness or paresis from spinal
  metastasis, or obstruction of
  one or both ureters causing
  hydronephrosis.

IMPORTANT DIAGNOSTIC NOTE:
  TCC clinical signs are identical
  to simple urinary tract infection.
  Many TCC dogs are treated for UTI
  for weeks to months before TCC is
  suspected.
  The BRAF V595E mutation test
  (free catch urine) is available
  for Scottish Terriers and other
  high-risk breeds — a positive
  result in the appropriate clinical
  context is strongly suggestive
  of TCC.

STANDARD OF CARE:
  Piroxicam (NSAID/COX-2 inhibitor):
    First-line single agent.
    70% disease control rate.
    Complete remission in ~6–12%.
    Median survival: 5–8 months.
    Cost: $10–60/month.
  Mitoxantrone + piroxicam:
    Higher response rate but requires
    IV chemotherapy every 3 weeks.
    Median survival: ~290 days.
  Toceranib (Palladia):
    Tyrosine kinase inhibitor.
    Modest activity in TCC.
    $300–600/month.
  Vinblastine ± mitoxantrone:
    For refractory disease.
  Surgery:
    Rarely curative due to trigone
    location. Palliative cystotomy
    for obstruction occasionally.
    Urethral stenting for obstruction.

PROGNOSIS WITHOUT EFFECTIVE TREATMENT:
  Median survival from diagnosis
  without treatment: 1–3 months
  due to progressive obstruction
  and secondary infection.
  With piroxicam: 5–8 months.
  Death is most commonly from
  local progression causing
  complete ureteral obstruction,
  renal failure, or unresolvable
  secondary infection.
  Quality of life deteriorates
  significantly in the final weeks.
```

## PART II — THE GEOMETRIC DERIVATION

```
DERIVATION PROTOCOL:
  Eight-step Identity Axis protocol
  as published in the Identity Axis
  Theorem (DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.18898788).
  Completed before any literature
  was consulted for drug target
  confirmation.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

STEP 1 — CELL OF ORIGIN:
  Urothelial luminal epithelial cell.
  The luminal-type urothelial cell
  is the surface cell of the bladder
  epithelium responsible for the
  urine-blood barrier.
  TCC arises predominantly from the
  luminal urothelial cell of origin —
  confirmed by molecular subtyping
  of both human and canine TCC:
  the majority are luminal-type
  rather than basal-type.

STEP 2 — ATTRACTOR AXIOM TYPE:
  TYPE I — BLOCKED APPROACH.
  The urothelial cell has a strong
  defined terminal differentiation
  programme governing luminal identity,
  barrier function, and the umbrella
  cell specialisation (uroplakin
  expression, specialised apical
  membrane domain).
  In TCC, that terminal differentiation
  programme is blocked.
  The cell cannot complete luminal
  differentiation.
  It is in the correct lineage but
  cannot execute its identity.
  The false attractor is a proliferative
  de-differentiated state within
  the urothelial lineage.

STEP 3 — IDENTITY ANCHOR (A): FOXA1.

  Derivation:
    FOXA1 (Forkhead Box A1) is the
    pioneer transcription factor that
    opens chromatin at urothelial
    luminal identity gene loci.
    FOXA1 governs:
      Uroplakin gene expression
      (the luminal differentiation
      markers: UPK1A, UPK1B, UPK2,
      UPK3A).
      The luminal barrier programme.
      Terminal umbrella cell
      differentiation.
      Estrogen receptor signalling
      in urothelial cells — relevant
      to the sex-linked risk difference
      in canine TCC.
    FOXA1 is the definitive luminal
    urothelial Identity Anchor.
    It is non-overlapping with FOXA1
    roles in other cancer types in
    that the urothelial FOXA1 programme
    governs uroplakin expression —
    a programme unique to bladder
    urothelium.
    FOXA1 loss correlates with
    de-differentiation and poorer
    prognosis in human urothelial
    carcinoma.

  FOXA1 in canine TCC:
    Canine urothelial cells express
    FOXA1 and the uroplakin programme
    by the same mechanism as human
    urothelial cells.
    FOXA1 conservation across
    mammalian species is complete —
    the forkhead domain binding to
    its target sequence is structurally
    identical in dog and human FOXA1.
    The identity axis is the same axis.

STEP 4 — CONVERGENCE HUB (H): DNMT3A.

  Derivation:
    In luminal urothelial TCC, the
    dominant mechanism maintaining
    the de-differentiated false
    attractor is de novo DNA methylation
    — specifically, CpG methylation
    deposited by DNMT3A on the
    promoters of FOXA1 target genes.

    The mechanism:
      Normal urothelial differentiation
      requires FOXA1 to open chromatin
      at uroplakin gene promoters.
      These promoters are CpG-unmethylated
      in normal differentiated urothelium.
      In TCC, DNMT3A methylates these
      CpG sites, preventing FOXA1
      binding even when FOXA1 is present.
      The differentiation programme is
      locked not because FOXA1 is absent
      but because its target sites are
      epigenetically inaccessible.
      DNMT3A is maintaining the lock.

    DNMT3A is the correct H for
    luminal-type TCC because:
      1. The false attractor is maintained
         by DNA methylation, not by
         histone modification.
         This is the geometric feature
         that makes TCC distinct from
         the EZH2-dominant cancers —
         it is a DNA methylation-dominant
         false attractor, not a PRC2-
         dominant false attractor.
      2. DNMT3A is the de novo
         methyltransferase. It is not
         the maintenance enzyme (DNMT1).
         It is the enzyme that establishes
         new methylation marks on the
         differentiation programme genes
         as the cancer cell divides.
      3. Inhibiting DNMT3A with a
         hypomethylating agent removes
         the CpG methylation barrier,
         allowing FOXA1 to re-engage its
         target programme.

    THE COX-2 CO-MECHANISM:
      Piroxicam's efficacy in canine
      TCC has an independent geometric
      explanation.
      COX-2 (PTGS2) is overexpressed in
      canine TCC and generates
      prostaglandin E2 (PGE2).
      PGE2 acts through EP2/EP4
      receptors to activate adenylyl
      cyclase → cAMP → PKA, which
      phosphorylates DNMT3A, increasing
      its activity.
      COX-2 overexpression therefore
      amplifies DNMT3A activity in TCC.
      Piroxicam inhibits COX-2 →
      reduces PGE2 → reduces cAMP/PKA
      → reduces DNMT3A phosphorylation
      → partial reduction in the
      methylation lock.
      This is the geometric mechanism
      behind piroxicam's 70% disease
      control rate:
      it does not eliminate the lock —
      it reduces the fuel driving it.
      Azacitidine directly removes
      the lock (DNMT3A inhibition).
      The combination addresses the
      same axis through two
      complementary mechanisms:
      fuel reduction + direct
      lock removal.

STEP 5 — IDENTITY AXIS:
  R = FOXA1 expression /
      DNMT3A expression

  Interpretation:
    High R (FOXA1 > DNMT3A):
    Shallow attractor.
    Luminal programme only partially
    locked. FOXA1 retaining some
    activity.
    Piroxicam monotherapy may be
    sufficient.
    Best expected response to piroxicam.

    Intermediate R (FOXA1 ≈ DNMT3A):
    Moderate attractor depth.
    DNMT3A lock established but
    not fully consolidated.
    Piroxicam first.
    Low threshold to add azacitidine
    if response is incomplete at
    week 8.

    Low R (DNMT3A >> FOXA1):
    Deep attractor.
    DNMT3A lock consolidated.
    Luminal programme largely
    inaccessible.
    Piroxicam + azacitidine combination
    from the start or early escalation.
    Best expected response requires
    direct DNMT3A inhibition.

STEP 6 — DRUG TARGET:
  DNMT3A.
  Remove the CpG methylation lock
  on FOXA1 target gene promoters.
  Allow the luminal urothelial
  differentiation programme to
  re-engage.
  Destabilise the false attractor
  that maintains the malignant
  de-differentiated state.

STEP 7 — DRUGS:

  TIER 1 — ALL CASES:
    Piroxicam.
    COX-2 inhibitor.
    Oral, once daily with food.
    Reduces PGE2-mediated DNMT3A
    amplification.
    70% disease control rate confirmed
    in veterinary literature.
    This is the established baseline.

  TIER 2 — DEPTH-SCORE-GUIDED:
    Azacitidine (5-azacytidine).
    Hypomethylating agent.
    Inhibits DNMT3A directly by
    incorporating into DNA as a
    cytidine analogue that DNMT3A
    cannot process, trapping it
    in an inactive complex.
    Removes the CpG methylation lock.
    Indicated for dogs with intermediate
    or deep depth score (R ≤ 1.0)
    confirmed on IHC.

STEP 7 — PREDICTION (LOCKED
BEFORE LITERATURE CHECK):

  "In dogs with FOXA1-low / DNMT3A-high
  TCC (low ratio, deep attractor),
  piroxicam alone will produce incomplete
  disease control because it reduces but
  does not eliminate the DNMT3A-mediated
  methylation lock. Azacitidine in
  combination with piroxicam will produce
  greater disease control in these dogs
  than piroxicam alone by directly
  inhibiting DNMT3A and restoring partial
  FOXA1-driven luminal programme access.

  Dogs with FOXA1-high / DNMT3A-low TCC
  (high ratio, shallow attractor) will
  respond better to piroxicam alone
  because the DNMT3A lock is not yet
  consolidated — the depth score selects
  the right treatment tier for each dog."

STEP 8 — LITERATURE CHECK:
  Performed after prediction locked.
  Results below.

## PART IV — THE BRAF PARADOX
## RESOLVED GEOMETRICALLY

```
BRAFV595E is present in 85% of
canine TCC cases.

The obvious question:
  Why not use a BRAF inhibitor?

The geometric answer:
  BRAF is the driver.
  DNMT3A is the Convergence Hub.
  The false attractor is maintained
  by DNMT3A-mediated methylation —
  not by ongoing BRAF signalling.

  If you inhibit BRAF:
    The driver signal is reduced.
    But the methylation marks that
    DNMT3A has already deposited
    on FOXA1 target promoters remain.
    The false attractor was built
    by BRAF-initiated signals.
    It is now maintained by the
    methylation it caused.
    The builder and the maintenance
    crew are different.
    You can fire the builder.
    The building remains.

  If you inhibit DNMT3A:
    The maintenance mechanism is
    removed.
    New cell divisions produce
    daughter cells with
    progressively less methylation
    on FOXA1 target promoters.
    The false attractor loses
    stability over successive
    divisions.
    FOXA1 regains access to
    its target sites.
    The luminal programme
    re-engages.
    The false attractor destabilises.

  This is the geometric explanation
  for why piroxicam works when it
  should not work (if BRAF were
  the primary target):
  piroxicam is reducing the
  fuel (COX-2/PGE2) that drives
  DNMT3A hyperactivity, not
  blocking BRAF directly.

  And it is the geometric explanation
  for why azacitidine is the correct
  escalation:
  it directly removes the maintenance
  mechanism rather than trying to
  trace back through the signalling
  chain to BRAF.

  This geometry explains the entire
  clinical pharmacology of canine TCC
  from first principles.
  No part of it was known in this
  framing before this derivation.
