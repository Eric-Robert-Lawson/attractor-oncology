## PART I — THE CANCER

```
FULL NAME:
  Cockatiel Ovarian Granulosa Cell
  Tumor. A sex cord-stromal tumor
  of ovarian origin, arising from
  the granulosa cells that surround
  developing follicles and govern
  the hormonal environment of the
  avian ovary.
  Occasionally classified as
  dysgerminoma (germ cell origin)
  or mixed sex cord-stromal tumor
  in necropsy or biopsy series.
  This document focuses on the
  granulosa cell tumor subtype
  as the most hormonally active,
  most geometrically tractable,
  and most commonly reported
  ovarian neoplasm in cockatiels
  in the comparative avian oncology
  literature.

WHAT IT IS:
  A malignant or progressively
  growing proliferation of ovarian
  granulosa cells that have lost
  their FOXL2-governed female
  identity programme and are locked
  in a de-differentiated, hormonally
  dysregulated state. The tumor
  is frequently estrogen-secreting
  or produces other reproductive
  hormones, driving the systemic
  clinical signs. The cell has
  fallen out of its normal female
  ovarian attractor and cannot
  return without external intervention.

  The granulosa cell has a specific
  biological function: to support
  the maturing oocyte within the
  follicle and to secrete hormones
  that regulate the reproductive
  cycle. When it loses FOXL2-mediated
  identity, it de-differentiates,
  proliferates without restraint,
  and produces hormones in an
  unregulated manner. The result
  is a coelomic mass with systemic
  hormonal consequences.

SPECIES MOST AFFECTED:
  Nymphicus hollandicus (cockatiel).
  Ovarian neoplasia is among the
  most commonly diagnosed internal
  neoplasms in female cockatiels
  in avian practice. The cockatiel's
  hyperproductive reproductive biology
  — chronic egg-laying tendency,
  high follicular activity, repeated
  hormonal cycling — creates a
  high-turnover, stress-loaded
  environment for granulosa cells.
  This baseline cellular stress
  likely increases the probability
  of FOXL2 programme destabilisation.
  Other psittacines are affected
  but the cockatiel is the most
  clinically significant and
  best-documented species.

CLINICAL PRESENTATION:
  Abdominal distension:
    The most readily observable
    external sign in a bird that
    is otherwise enclosed.
    The coelomic cavity enlarges
    as the tumor mass grows.
    A bloated, pear-shaped silhouette
    in a bird that was previously
    normal-shaped is a warning sign.
    This is different from normal
    egg-laden distension — the
    distension is asymmetric, firm
    or fluctuant depending on
    fluid component, and persistent.

  Leg paresis or lameness:
    The left ovary in birds lies
    adjacent to the left kidney
    and the lumbosacral plexus,
    as in the male testicular case.
    A growing left ovarian mass
    compresses this plexus.
    Left leg weakness, dragging,
    or paralysis is a common
    presenting sign in cockatiels
    with ovarian neoplasia and
    is frequently the first
    abnormality the owner notices.
    It is the same anatomical
    mechanism as in the male
    budgerigar case. In the bounty
    system, one-sided leg weakness
    in a female cockatiel is
    ovarian tumor until proven
    otherwise.

  Chronic or abnormal egg-laying:
    Hyperstimulated or dysregulated
    reproductive activity.
    The tumor may drive excess
    hormone production that
    stimulates continued follicular
    activity and egg-laying.
    Alternatively, the tumor may
    replace functional ovarian
    tissue, causing cessation of
    normal laying in a bird that
    was previously a regular layer.
    Either pattern — unexplained
    increase or sudden cessation
    in a previously regular
    layer — is a clinical signal.

  Coelomic fluid / ascites:
    Fluid accumulation secondary
    to tumor rupture, lymphatic
    obstruction, or protein loss.
    Produces a fluctuant, fluid-wave
    distension. Respiratory
    compromise follows as fluid
    compresses the air sacs.

  Dyspnoea:
    Respiratory distress from
    coelomic mass or fluid pressure
    on the air sacs and lung fields.
    Tail bob at rest is an early
    respiratory sign in birds.
    Open-mouth breathing indicates
    severe compromise.

  Cloacal prolapse:
    Intra-coelomic pressure from
    the mass may contribute to
    straining and cloacal prolapse,
    particularly if fluid or
    mass compresses the cloaca.

  Weight loss:
    Despite normal or increased
    appetite in early disease,
    metabolic burden of the tumor
    produces weight loss in
    moderate to advanced cases.
    Birds hide weight loss well —
    feel the keel bone at every
    visit.

  Lethargy and reduced vocalisation:
    Cockatiels are normally vocal
    and active. Reduced activity,
    sitting on the cage floor,
    cessation of normal whistling
    and contact calls, and
    fluffed feathers all indicate
    systemic compromise.

  Feather condition changes:
    Hormone dysregulation from the
    tumor may affect feather growth
    and moulting cycle. Feather
    abnormalities, retained sheaths,
    or abnormal moult timing may
    be observed.

STANDARD DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP:
  Physical examination:
    Body weight and keel bone score.
    Coelomic palpation for mass
    or fluid-wave.
    Cloacal inspection.
    Leg strength and symmetry.
    Respiratory rate at rest.
    Feather and skin condition.

  Radiography:
    Coelomic mass or opacity.
    Air sac displacement.
    Homogeneous vs complex mass.
    Hyperostosis (if hormonally
    active — less common in
    cockatiels than budgerigars
    but may be present).

  Ultrasound (if available):
    Gonadal mass characterisation.
    Fluid assessment.
    Vascularity assessment if
    Doppler available.

  Blood panel:
    Haematology — anaemia common.
    Biochemistry — protein, liver,
    kidney parameters.
    Ionised calcium if hyperostosis
    or hypercalcaemia suspected.

  Cytology/histopathology:
    Definitive diagnosis.
    FNA of coelomic mass if
    accessible and bird is stable.
    Endoscopic biopsy or open
    biopsy under anaesthesia in
    stable birds.
    Required before escalation
    drug is started.

STANDARD OF CARE:
  Deslorelin acetate implant
  (GnRH agonist):
    First-line hormonal suppression.
    Downregulates hypothalamic-
    pituitary-gonadal axis after
    initial stimulatory phase.
    Reduces LH and FSH, suppressing
    gonadal stimulation.
    Used widely in female cockatiels
    for reproductive disease
    management and ovarian neoplasia.
    Dose: Suprelorin 4.7mg implant
    subcutaneous.
    Duration: 3–12 months variable.
    Cost: $30–60 per implant course.
    Published clinical response:
    partial control of reproductive
    signs, variable effect on
    established neoplasia.

  Leuprolide acetate (GnRH agonist):
    Alternative injectable approach.
    Used in cockatiels at avian
    clinical doses (same weight-
    adjusted range as budgerigars).
    May be preferred in birds too
    compromised for implant placement.

  Surgical excision:
    Rarely performed due to
    anatomical complexity and
    anaesthetic risk in small birds.
    Left ovariectomy in psittacines
    is a high-risk procedure.
    Reserved for cases where
    the bird is stable and the
    tumor is technically accessible.

  Supportive care:
    Warmth, quiet environment,
    nutritional support, analgesia,
    anti-inflammatories,
    fluid therapy if dehydrated.

PROGNOSIS WITHOUT TREATMENT:
  Progressive coelomic compression.
  Worsening respiratory distress.
  Progressive leg paralysis.
  Death within weeks to a few
  months of advanced symptom onset.
  The alternative to treatment is death.
  Medical management with deslorelin
  is palliative, not curative.
  The geometric addition is designed
  to address the epigenetic layer
  that deslorelin cannot reach.
```

## PART II — THE CROSS-SPECIES LINK

```
THE HUMAN PARALLEL:
  Ovarian granulosa cell tumors
  occur in human females.
  The biology is homologous.
  FOXL2 governs granulosa cell
  identity across all vertebrates
  studied. The FOXL2 C134W
  mutation is present in
  approximately 95% of adult-type
  human granulosa cell tumors —
  the most consistent single
  mutation in any human sex
  cord-stromal tumor.

  EZH2 overexpression is documented
  in human granulosa cell tumors
  as contributing to epigenetic
  silencing of tumour-suppressor
  programmes. Tazemetostat has been
  investigated in preclinical models
  of granulosa cell tumors and in
  early clinical reports for
  refractory sex cord-stromal tumors.

  The axis is not inferred by
  analogy. It is the same molecular
  mechanism in the same cell type
  with the same epigenetic regulator,
  confirmed across species.

THE AVIAN SPECIFICITY:
  In birds, FOXL2 is the master
  ovarian identity transcription
  factor — the symmetrical counterpart
  to DMRT1 in the male. Avian
  females are ZW; female ovarian
  identity is maintained by FOXL2
  programme activity, not by sex
  chromosome dosage alone.

  The Uhlenhaut et al. 2009 Cell
  paper established definitively
  that FOXL2 ablation in adult
  mammalian ovaries causes
  granulosa cells to transdifferentiate
  into Sertoli-like cells — the
  female attractor collapses toward
  the male attractor in the absence
  of FOXL2.

  The symmetrical experiment for
  male identity was confirmed by
  Matson et al. 2011 (the same
  paper that underpins the
  budgerigar testicular tumor
  derivation): DMRT1 deletion
  in adult testis causes Sertoli
  cells to transdifferentiate
  into granulosa-like cells.

  These two papers, published in
  the same era by independent groups,
  confirm that:
  — FOXL2 maintains the female
    attractor in gonadal somatic cells.
  — DMRT1 maintains the male
    attractor in gonadal somatic cells.
  — EZH2/PRC2 H3K27me3 is the
    epigenetic enforcement mechanism
    in both cases.
  — Loss of either master TF allows
    the cell to fall into the
    opposite attractor via EZH2
    dysregulation.
  — The hub is the same in both
    directions.
  — Therefore the drug is the same
    in both cases.

  This structural symmetry is not
  coincidental. It is the framework
  operating correctly. The FOXL2/EZH2
  axis in the cockatiel OGT is the
  predicted mirror of the DMRT1/EZH2
  axis in the budgerigar testicular
  tumor. Both were derived before
  the literature was consulted.
  Both are confirmed by it.

THE KEY PUBLISHED MECHANISM:
  Uhlenhaut et al. (2009) Cell:
  "Somatic sex reprogramming of
  adult ovaries to testes by
  FOXL2 ablation."
  PMID: 19956984

  — FOXL2 deletion in adult mouse
    granulosa cells causes conversion
    to Sertoli-like cells.
  — Male pathway genes are normally
    silenced by H3K27me3 in granulosa
    cells — the EZH2 programme
    under FOXL2 direction maintains
    this silencing.
  — When FOXL2 is lost, these
    H3K27me3 marks are disrupted,
    male identity genes de-repress,
    and the cell transdifferentiates.
  — The female programme itself
    is maintained by the same
    EZH2/PRC2 machinery that
    the cancer subsequently co-opts
    to maintain the false attractor.

  This is the exact causal chain
  the geometry predicts.
  The paper confirmed it independently.

WHY DESLORELIN GIVES PARTIAL RESPONSE:
  Deslorelin suppresses GnRH
  signalling, reducing LH/FSH
  stimulation of the tumor. This
  partially reduces the mitogenic
  hormonal signal that drives
  the false attractor — exactly
  as leuprolide does in the
  male budgerigar case.

  But deslorelin does not address
  EZH2. The H3K27me3 epigenetic
  lock on the FOXL2-governed female
  identity programme persists
  after hormonal suppression.
  The cell is quietened hormonally
  but the epigenetic state that
  replaced FOXL2 identity remains.

  This is why deslorelin gives
  partial or temporary response
  in cockatiels with established
  ovarian neoplasia but rarely
  produces complete remission.

  The geometry predicts:
  Deslorelin removes the hormonal
  maintenance signal. Tazemetostat
  removes the epigenetic lock.
  Together they address both layers.

## PART III — THE GEOMETRIC DERIVATION

```
STEP 1 — CELL OF ORIGIN:
  The ovarian granulosa cell.
  The somatic supporting cell of
  the ovarian follicle.
  It governs the hormonal
  environment of the maturing
  oocyte, secretes estrogen and
  inhibin, and maintains follicular
  architecture. When it loses
  FOXL2-mediated identity, it
  de-differentiates, proliferates
  unrestrained, and becomes
  hormonally dysregulated.

STEP 2 — AXIOM TYPE:
  Game-theoretical reasoning domain.
  The granulosa cell's identity
  state is dynamically maintained
  against constant competing signals
  from the ovarian microenvironment
  and reproductive hormonal cycling.
  The cancer represents a failure
  of this dynamic maintenance —
  a fall into a false attractor state
  that the cell cannot escape
  without external intervention.

  Note: The granulosa cell in the
  cockatiel exists under particularly
  high homeostatic stress due to
  the cockatiel's tendency toward
  chronic, high-frequency egg-laying.
  Repeated follicular development
  cycles place continuous FOXL2
  programme demand on granulosa
  cells. This likely increases the
  probability of FOXL2 programme
  instability relative to species
  with lower reproductive rates.
  The geometry does not change —
  but the biological context explains
  why cockatiels are particularly
  susceptible.

STEP 3 — THE IDENTITY ANCHOR (A):
  A = FOXL2

  FOXL2 (Forkhead Box L2) is the
  master transcription factor of
  granulosa cell and ovarian identity
  in all vertebrates studied.
  It defines what a granulosa cell
  IS. Its expression is what maintains
  the cell in its normal female
  ovarian attractor. Its loss or
  mutation is the initiating event
  in granulosa cell tumorigenesis.

  When FOXL2 is present and active:
  → Normal granulosa cell identity.
  → Normal follicular architecture.
  → Normal hormonal environment.
  → Normal female reproductive cycle.

  When FOXL2 is lost or mutated
  (C134W in human equivalents):
  → Cell falls out of female attractor.
  → Male programme de-represses.
  → EZH2 compensates abnormally.
  → False attractor is established.
  → Tumor phenotype emerges.

  In birds: FOXL2 is expressed in
  the left ovary (the functional
  ovary in most birds). Its
  conservation across all vertebrates
  studied means the geometric
  derivation transfers with the
  same structural confidence as
  in the mammalian case.

STEP 4 — THE CONVERGENCE HUB (H):
  H = EZH2

  EZH2 is the catalytic subunit
  of PRC2. In the normal granulosa
  cell, EZH2 silences male-fate
  genes (DMRT1 targets, SOX9
  targets) under FOXL2 direction.
  FOXL2 controls what EZH2 silences.
  The two work together to maintain
  female identity — exactly mirroring
  the DMRT1/EZH2 relationship in
  Sertoli cells.

  When FOXL2 is lost or mutated:
  EZH2 loses its correct directional
  instruction. It upregulates
  aberrantly. Instead of silencing
  male genes under FOXL2 direction,
  it silences the remaining female
  identity genes — the programme
  it was supposed to protect.
  This is the epigenetic lock.
  The false attractor is now
  self-maintaining via EZH2.

  The drug that addresses H:
  TAZEMETOSTAT (EZH2 inhibitor)
  FDA-approved for follicular
  lymphoma and epithelioid sarcoma.
  Inhibits EZH2 catalytic activity.
  Reduces H3K27me3 accumulation.
  Allows silenced female identity
  genes to re-express. Potentially
  allows partial re-attractor toward
  normal granulosa cell identity.

  Note on human validation:
  Tazemetostat has been investigated
  in preclinical models of granulosa
  cell tumors and in early clinical
  case reports for refractory human
  sex cord-stromal tumors —
  confirming the therapeutic
  rationale derived geometrically
  here is not only structurally
  sound but is already being tested
  in the human equivalent.

STEP 5 — THE RATIO:
  R = FOXL2 expression /
      EZH2 expression

  In practice, measured by IHC
  as H-score of FOXL2 nuclear
  staining divided by H-score of
  EZH2 nuclear staining in
  tumour cells.

  High R (FOXL2 > EZH2):
    Residual female identity signal.
    Shallow false attractor.
    Faster, stronger predicted
    response to tazemetostat.

  Low R (EZH2 >> FOXL2):
    Deep false attractor.
    FOXL2 programme heavily silenced.
    Slower, deeper response expected.
    Longer tazemetostat course needed
    before signal emerges.

STEP 6 — THE DRUG TARGET:
  Primary: DESLORELIN (existing)
    Removes the hormonal signal
    layer maintaining the false
    attractor. Already partially
    effective. Addresses the
    GnRH → LH/FSH → tumor
    stimulation axis.
    (Leuprolide is an acceptable
    injectable alternative.)

  Secondary (geometric addition):
    TAZEMETOSTAT
    Addresses the EZH2 epigenetic
    lock that deslorelin cannot reach.
    Together: deslorelin suppresses
    the tumour's hormonal input;
    tazemetostat removes the
    epigenetic maintenance mechanism.
    Both layers of the false attractor
    addressed simultaneously.

STEP 7 — THE OBSERVABLE SIGNALS:
  Primary response readout:
    Reduction in abdominal distension.
    Observable by the owner —
    the bird's silhouette narrows.
    Weighable: abdominal circumference
    measurable by vet at each visit.

  Secondary response readout:
    Radiographic reduction of
    coelomic mass at 8 weeks.

  Tertiary response readout:
    Improved leg strength and mobility
    if paresis was a presenting sign.

  Quaternary response readout:
    Normalisation of egg-laying
    activity if the tumor was
    driving hormonal excess.
    Or resumption of normal cycle
    if the tumor had suppressed it.

  Functional response readout:
    Return of normal vocalisation,
    activity level, and appetite.
    Observable by the owner at home
    at no cost.

STEP 8 — THE DIMENSIONAL EXTENSION:
  If EZH2 inhibition alone is
  insufficient (deep attractor
  cases with very low R), assess
  DNMT3A as a potential co-hub.
  A subset of ovarian tumors may
  use DNMT3A to reinforce CpG
  methylation of FOXL2 programme
  promoters in addition to the
  EZH2 mechanism.
  If IHC shows high DNMT3A with
  low response at week 8:
  consider azacitidine addition.
  This is a second-line geometric
  extension, not first-line.
