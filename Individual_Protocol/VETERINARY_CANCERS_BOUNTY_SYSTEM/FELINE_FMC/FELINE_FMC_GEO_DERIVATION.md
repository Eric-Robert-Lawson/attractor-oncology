## PART I — THE CANCER

```
FULL NAME:
  Feline Mammary Carcinoma (FMC).
  Also: Feline Mammary Adenocarcinoma,
  Feline Breast Cancer.
  The third most common cancer in
  female cats. 90% of feline mammary
  tumours are malignant — this is
  strikingly higher than dogs (50%
  malignant) and differs from the
  human distribution.

WHAT IT IS:
  A malignant neoplasm arising from
  mammary epithelial cells. Cats have
  4 pairs of mammary glands (8 total).
  Tumours frequently arise in multiple
  glands simultaneously or sequentially.
  The biology is aggressive: metastasis
  to regional lymph nodes and lung
  is common and early.

  The two geometric subtypes have
  different biology and different
  treatment logic:

  LUMINAL FMC:
    Retains ER/FOXA1-governed luminal
    identity programme in suppressed form.
    EZH2 locks the programme, but the
    identity anchor is present.
    Tazemetostat dissolves the EZH2 lock,
    restoring endocrine sensitivity.
    Endocrine therapy (toremifene or
    megestrol acetate in cats, or
    ovariohysterectomy in intact cats)
    may become effective after EZH2
    lock dissolution.
    Better baseline prognosis than TNBC.
    Tumours < 2cm: median survival
    3–4 years. Tumours > 3cm: 6–12 months.

  TRIPLE-NEGATIVE FMC:
    No ER, PR, or HER2.
    EZH2 is the dominant hub maintaining
    the de-differentiated basal-like state.
    TP63 or residual luminal programme
    is the relevant identity context.
    No endocrine therapy applicable.
    No HER2-targeted therapy applicable.
    No standard targeted therapy exists.
    Tazemetostat is the first geometry-
    derived targeted therapy for this
    subtype in cats.
    This is the worst-prognosis group.
    It is also the largest group
    (~50–70% of all FMC).
    Chemotherapy (doxorubicin) has
    modest and inconsistent benefit.
    Median survival for large TNBC-FMC:
    6–12 months.

CLINICAL PRESENTATION:
  Palpable mammary mass — single or
  multiple. Any gland pair, though
  caudal glands (inguinal) are
  slightly more common.
  Skin over mass: may be adherent,
  erythematous, ulcerated.
  Peau d'orange appearance in
  inflammatory carcinoma (rare, very
  aggressive, dermal lymphatic invasion).
  Regional lymphadenopathy (axillary,
  inguinal): indicates nodal metastasis.
  Constitutional signs in advanced
  disease: weight loss, dyspnoea
  (pulmonary metastasis), lethargy.
  Owners often discover as incidental
  finding during grooming.

  Multifocality is common — always
  examine all glands bilaterally.
  Tumours in multiple glands may have
  different histological grades and
  different IHC profiles — the worst
  subtype drives treatment decisions.

BREEDS AT RISK:
  Siamese: highest risk. Earlier onset
  (median 9 years vs 14 years in
  other breeds). Higher rate of
  lymphatic invasion. More aggressive
  biological behaviour.
  Domestic Shorthair: most common
  breed presenting simply due to
  population size.
  Persian, Burmese: some studies
  report overrepresentation.
  Entire (unspayed) females: highest
  risk. Spaying before 6 months
  reduces lifetime risk by 91%.
  Spaying at 6–12 months: reduces
  by 86%. After first heat: much less
  protective.

INCIDENCE:
  Third most common feline cancer.
  17% of all tumours in female cats.
  ~25,000–50,000 estimated new cases
  per year in the US (estimates vary —
  feline cancer registry data less
  complete than canine).
  90% malignant (contrast: dogs 50%).
  This 90% malignancy rate makes FMC
  the most reliably malignant mammary
  tumour among commonly treated
  companion animal species.

STANDARD OF CARE:
  Surgery:
    Unilateral or bilateral radical
    mastectomy (full chain removal)
    is preferred over lumpectomy.
    Radical mastectomy reduces local
    recurrence and may marginally
    improve survival.
    Staged bilateral mastectomy for
    bilateral disease.
    Axillary and inguinal lymph node
    dissection when enlarged.
    Surgery is the ONLY treatment with
    proven survival benefit in FMC.

  Chemotherapy:
    Doxorubicin (with or without
    cyclophosphamide):
    Benefit is controversial.
    2024 systematic review: only 1/5
    studies showed survival advantage
    when adjuvant chemotherapy was
    added to surgery.
    Used in practice for high-risk
    cases (large tumour, incomplete
    excision, lymph node positive).
    Response rate modest. Median
    survival extension uncertain.

  Cyclooxygenase inhibitors (piroxicam,
  meloxicam):
    Some anti-tumour activity in FMC.
    Palliative pain management.
    Meloxicam: careful dose selection
    required in cats.

  Endocrine therapy:
    Toremifene (selective ER modulator):
    Used in some European practices
    for ER+ FMC. Limited published
    evidence but biologically rational
    for luminal subtype.
    Megestrol acetate: CONTRAINDICATED
    in cats with mammary carcinoma —
    progestogens promote mammary
    tumour growth.
    Ovariohysterectomy at time of
    mastectomy: recommended for intact
    female cats — removes hormonal
    stimulation. No proven survival
    benefit in controlled trials but
    standard practice.

  Radiation:
    Rarely used in FMC. No established
    protocol. Palliative for non-
    resectable disease.

WHAT STANDARD OF CARE DOES NOT
ADDRESS:
  The EZH2 epigenetic lock in either
  subtype.

  In LUMINAL FMC:
    Surgery removes the primary tumour.
    Endocrine therapy targets the ER
    programme — but in EZH2-locked
    luminal cells, ER target genes are
    H3K27me3-silenced, making endocrine
    therapy less effective.
    The lock must be dissolved before
    endocrine therapy can work.
    Tazemetostat dissolves the lock.
    Endocrine therapy then acts on
    the de-repressed ER programme.

  In TRIPLE-NEGATIVE FMC:
    No targeted therapy exists.
    Chemotherapy kills proliferating
    cells but does not address the
    EZH2 lock.
    Residual TNBC-FMC cells that
    survive chemotherapy retain the
    EZH2 lock and re-expand.
    Tazemetostat is the first
    geometry-derived mechanism-targeted
    agent for this subtype.
```

```
     cut-points for human tissue.
     The feline FMC case produces
     evidence that the same biology
     operates across species.

     These are different studies with
     additive evidential weight.
     A human oncologist reading the
     feline case report will
     understand immediately why it
     matters for their human patients.

  2. THE DOMINANT SUBTYPE (TN-FMC)
     HAS NO TARGETED THERAPY.

     50–70% of cats with FMC have
     triple-negative disease with
     no targeted treatment option.
     Chemotherapy benefit is modest
     and inconsistent. Median survival
     for large TNBC-FMC: 6–12 months.

     Tazemetostat is the first
     geometry-derived mechanism-
     targeted agent for this subtype.
     Every TNBC-FMC cat that receives
     tazemetostat under this protocol
     is contributing to evidence that
     currently does not exist for
     the EZH2 inhibitor approach
     in feline TNBC.

     The parallel to human TNBC
     (same molecular deficit, same
     EZH2 geometry, same lack of
     targeted therapy) is the human
     translational argument.

  3. THE FOXA1 ANTIBODY CONSTRAINT
     IS RESOLVABLE IN ONE EXPERIMENT.

     The absence of commercial feline
     FOXA1 antibody validation is a
     one-experiment constraint.
     Take a known FOXA1-positive
     human control, a known canine
     mammary tissue, and a feline
     mammary tissue from an existing
     archive. Run D4E2 (CST #53528)
     and clone 3A8 (Thermo Fisher)
     on all three. Compare staining
     patterns.

     If feline tissue shows nuclear
     staining in luminal cells
     consistent with human and canine
     controls: validated. Report the
     validation in the case report
     as a supplementary finding.
     This is a half-day experiment
     at any veterinary pathology
     reference lab with the right
     tissue archives.

  4. THE FELINE PHARMACOLOGY CONSTRAINT
     IS A FEATURE, NOT A BUG.

     The fact that cats require more
     conservative dosing and more
     intensive monitoring means:
     (a) The initial case reports
     will be highly monitored and
     well-documented — which is
     better science.
     (b) If tazemetostat is well-
     tolerated in cats at conservative
     doses, it establishes a safety
     floor for the species that
     currently does not exist in
     the literature.
     (c) Published feline tazemetostat
     PK/safety data from case reports
     is itself a publishable finding
     independent of the anti-tumour
     response.

  5. SIAMESE CATS AND THE SPECIFIC
     POPULATION.

     The Siamese breed overrepresentation
     with earlier onset and more
     aggressive biological behaviour
     makes Siamese FMC cases
     particularly high-priority for
     this protocol. Earlier onset
     means younger cats with longer
     potential benefit horizon from
     an effective targeted therapy.
     More aggressive biology means
     the population where standard
     of care fails most clearly —
     which is where the geometry-
     directed approach has the most
     visible impact.
```
