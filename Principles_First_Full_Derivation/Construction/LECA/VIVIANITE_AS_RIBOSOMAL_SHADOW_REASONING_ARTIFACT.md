### 2.1 Mars Was More Phosphate-Rich
### Than Early Earth

This is the finding that changes the
weight of the vivianite signal considerably.

Adcock, Hausrath, and Forster (2013,
Nature Geoscience, DOI: 10.1038/ngeo1923)
analyzed Martian meteorites and found:

```
Martian basalt phosphate content:
  5-10 times higher than typical
  early Earth basalts.

Mechanism:
  Martian mantle conditions (lower
  oxygen fugacity, higher chlorine)
  favoured incorporation of
  chlorapatite and merrillite —
  minerals more soluble under
  acidic, low-temperature conditions
  than terrestrial apatite.

Consequence:
  Water interacting with Martian
  basalt released phosphate more
  readily than water interacting
  with early Earth basalt.

Estimated phosphate concentration
in early Martian lakes:
  Potentially 10-100x higher than
  early Earth ocean phosphate.
  Micromolar to millimolar range
  plausible.
```

**This is not a minor detail.**

Early Earth phosphate availability
was a limiting factor for life.
It constrained how densely packed
the early biosphere could be.

Mars did not have this constraint.

A LUCA-grade biosphere on Mars
had MORE phosphate available per
unit of water than Earth life did.

More phosphate = more RNA synthesis.
More RNA = more ribosomes.
More ribosomes = denser, more active
biological communities.
Denser communities = stronger
phosphate cycling signal in the
mineral record when cells die.

### 2.2 The Ribosome as Phosphate Sink

Ribosomes are the most phosphate-dense
structure in any living cell.

```
Cellular composition of a rapidly
growing prokaryotic cell (E. coli
as reference organism):

Ribosomes:
  Up to 40% of total cell dry mass.
  Two-thirds rRNA by mass.
  One-third protein by mass.

Total RNA:
  15-20% of cell dry mass.
  80-85% of cellular RNA is rRNA.
  10-15% is tRNA.
  1-5% is mRNA.

Phosphate content of rRNA:
  ~10% of rRNA mass.
  (Phosphate backbone of RNA polymer.)

Therefore:
  ~12-16% of cell dry mass is rRNA.
  ~10% of that is phosphate.
  Ribosomal phosphate alone represents
  ~1.5-2% of total cell dry mass.

In a community of 10⁸ cells per
millilitre (typical dense microbial
mat density):
  The phosphate released when those
  cells lyse is enormous relative
  to the water volume.
```
